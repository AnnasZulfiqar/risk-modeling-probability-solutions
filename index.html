Question 1

Complete question. Let \(X\) be a standard normal random variable, meaning that \(X\) has mean \(0\) and variance \(1\).

  1. Find the density of \(Y=X^2\).
  2. Find the density of \(Z=1/Y\).
  3. Determine whether \(E[Z]\) is finite or not.
  4. If \(X_1,X_2\) are independent standard normal random variables and \(p\in(0,1)\), show that \[ \frac{1}{\left(\sqrt{p}X_1+\sqrt{1-p}X_2\right)^2} \] has the same distribution as \(Z\) above.

Topic and purpose. This question tests change of variables for densities, tail behavior of an expectation, and the fact that linear combinations of independent normal random variables are normal.

Rules used. A standard normal variable has density \[ f_X(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2},\qquad -\infty\lt x\lt\infty. \] If \(Y=g(X)\) and the equation \(g(x)=y\) has roots \(x_i\), the change-of-variables formula is \[ f_Y(y)=\sum_i \frac{f_X(x_i)}{|g'(x_i)|}. \] The derivative \(g'(x)\) tells us how much the transformation stretches the \(x\)-axis.

(a) Density of \(Y=X^2\)

\[ y=x^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x=\sqrt{y}\ \text{or}\ x=-\sqrt{y}. \]

For a positive value \(y\), two possible \(x\)-values produce it, because both a positive and a negative number square to the same positive number.

\[ g(x)=x^2,\qquad g'(x)=2x. \]

We calculate the derivative because the transformation formula divides by the absolute value of this derivative.

\[ |g'(\sqrt{y})|=2\sqrt{y},\qquad |g'(-\sqrt{y})|=2\sqrt{y}. \]

The absolute value removes the minus sign from \(2(-\sqrt{y})\).

\[ f_Y(y)=\frac{f_X(\sqrt{y})}{2\sqrt{y}}+\frac{f_X(-\sqrt{y})}{2\sqrt{y}},\qquad y\gt0. \]

We add both contributions because either root can give the same value \(Y=y\).

\[ f_X(\sqrt{y})=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y/2}, \qquad f_X(-\sqrt{y})=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y/2}. \]

In both cases \(x^2=y\), so the exponential part is the same.

\[ f_Y(y)=\frac{2\cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y/2}}{2\sqrt{y}} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi y}}e^{-y/2},\qquad y\gt0. \]

The factor \(2\) in the numerator cancels the factor \(2\) in the denominator.

PDF check. The density matches the PDF answer: \(f_Y(y)=y^{-1/2}e^{-y/2}/\sqrt{2\pi}\) for \(y\gt0\).

(b) Density of \(Z=1/Y\)

\[ z=\frac{1}{y}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad y=\frac{1}{z}. \]

Because \(Y\gt0\), we also have \(Z\gt0\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dz}=-\frac{1}{z^2}, \qquad \left|\frac{dy}{dz}\right|=\frac{1}{z^2}. \]

For a one-to-one transformation, the new density is the old density at the inverse value times this absolute derivative.

\[ f_Z(z)=f_Y(1/z)\frac{1}{z^2}. \]

We substitute \(y=1/z\) into the density of \(Y\).

\[ f_Y(1/z)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi(1/z)}}e^{-1/(2z)} =\frac{\sqrt{z}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-1/(2z)}. \]

Since \(\sqrt{1/z}=1/\sqrt{z}\), dividing by \(\sqrt{1/z}\) is the same as multiplying by \(\sqrt{z}\).

\[ f_Z(z)=\frac{\sqrt{z}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-1/(2z)}\cdot \frac{1}{z^2} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi z^3}}e^{-1/(2z)},\qquad z\gt0. \]

We combine \(z^{1/2}\) and \(z^{-2}\): \(z^{1/2-2}=z^{-3/2}\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF density \(f_Z(z)=e^{-1/(2z)}/\sqrt{2\pi z^3}\) for \(z\gt0\).

(c) Is \(E[Z]\) finite?

\[ E[Z]=\int_0^\infty z f_Z(z)\,dz. \]

For a positive continuous random variable, expectation is found by integrating \(z\) times its density.

\[ zf_Z(z)=z\cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi z^3}}e^{-1/(2z)} =\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}z^{-1/2}e^{-1/(2z)}. \]

We simplify \(z/z^{3/2}=z^{-1/2}\).

\[ \lim_{z\to\infty} e^{-1/(2z)}=e^0=1. \]

For large \(z\), the exponential factor is close to \(1\), so the integrand behaves like a constant times \(z^{-1/2}\).

\[ \int_M^\infty z^{-1/2}\,dz =\left[2z^{1/2}\right]_M^\infty =+\infty. \]

The comparison integral diverges, so the expectation of \(Z\) diverges too.

PDF check. The PDF says \(E[Z]\) is infinite. Our conclusion matches.

(d) Distribution of the reciprocal square of a normal combination

\[ A=\sqrt{p}X_1+\sqrt{1-p}X_2. \]

We name the expression inside the square \(A\) so that the target variable becomes \(1/A^2\).

\[ E[A]=\sqrt{p}E[X_1]+\sqrt{1-p}E[X_2]=0. \]

Both \(X_1\) and \(X_2\) have mean \(0\), so the weighted sum also has mean \(0\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}(A) =p\operatorname{Var}(X_1)+(1-p)\operatorname{Var}(X_2) =p+(1-p)=1. \]

Independence means there is no covariance term. Each standard normal has variance \(1\).

\[ A\sim N(0,1). \]

A linear combination of independent normal variables is normal. With mean \(0\) and variance \(1\), it is standard normal.

\[ A^2 \stackrel{d}{=} X^2=Y, \qquad \frac{1}{A^2}\stackrel{d}{=}\frac{1}{Y}=Z. \]

The symbol \(\stackrel{d}{=}\) means "has the same distribution as". Since \(A\) and \(X\) are both standard normal, their squares have the same distribution, and their reciprocal squares also have the same distribution.

Common mistakes. Forgetting the negative root in part (a), losing the absolute value in a change of variables, and thinking that an infinite expectation means the density is invalid. The density is valid; only the mean is infinite.

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

Question 2

Complete question. Let \(X_t\) take values in \((0,1)\) and satisfy the stochastic differential equation

\[ dX_t=\kappa(\theta-X_t)\,dt+\sigma\sqrt{X_t(1-X_t)}\,dW_t, \qquad X_0=x. \]
  1. Show that \(m(t):=E[X_t]\) satisfies \(m'(t)=\kappa(\theta-m(t))\).
  2. Find an explicit expression for \(m(t)\).
  3. Use Ito's formula to find the SDE for \(Y_t=X_t^2\).
  4. Use the previous point to obtain a differential equation for \(s(t):=E[X_t^2]\).

Topic and purpose. This question tests how to take expectations in an SDE, solve a first-order linear ordinary differential equation, and use Ito's formula.

Rules used. A stochastic integral \(\int_0^t H_s\,dW_s\) has expectation \(0\) when the integrand is well behaved. Ito's formula says that for \(Y_t=f(X_t)\), \[ dY_t=f'(X_t)dX_t+\frac12 f''(X_t)(dX_t)^2. \] Also, \((dW_t)^2=dt\), while \(dt\,dW_t=0\) and \((dt)^2=0\).

(a) Differential equation for \(m(t)\)

\[ X_t=x+\int_0^t \kappa(\theta-X_u)\,du +\int_0^t \sigma\sqrt{X_u(1-X_u)}\,dW_u. \]

This is the integral form of the SDE: add the accumulated drift and accumulated Brownian noise to the initial value.

\[ E[X_t]=x+E\left[\int_0^t \kappa(\theta-X_u)\,du\right] +E\left[\int_0^t \sigma\sqrt{X_u(1-X_u)}\,dW_u\right]. \]

We take expectation of both sides.

\[ E\left[\int_0^t \sigma\sqrt{X_u(1-X_u)}\,dW_u\right]=0. \]

The stochastic integral has mean zero. The condition \(0\lt X_u\lt1\) makes the square-root factor bounded.

\[ m(t)=x+\int_0^t \kappa(\theta-E[X_u])\,du =x+\int_0^t \kappa(\theta-m(u))\,du. \]

Expectation is linear, so \(E[\theta-X_u]=\theta-E[X_u]\).

\[ m'(t)=\kappa(\theta-m(t)). \]

Differentiating the integral equation gives the required ordinary differential equation.

PDF check. This is exactly the equation stated in the PDF.

(b) Explicit expression for \(m(t)\)

\[ m'(t)=\kappa\theta-\kappa m(t) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad m'(t)+\kappa m(t)=\kappa\theta. \]

We move the term involving \(m(t)\) to the left. This is a linear ODE.

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{\kappa t}m(t)\right) =e^{\kappa t}\bigl(m'(t)+\kappa m(t)\bigr). \]

The integrating factor is \(e^{\kappa t}\). It turns the left side into a single derivative.

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{\kappa t}m(t)\right)=\kappa\theta e^{\kappa t}. \]

We substitute the ODE into the previous line.

\[ e^{\kappa t}m(t)-m(0)=\int_0^t \kappa\theta e^{\kappa u}\,du =\theta(e^{\kappa t}-1). \]

We integrate both sides from \(0\) to \(t\). Since \(m(0)=E[X_0]=x\), the initial value is \(x\).

\[ e^{\kappa t}m(t)=x+\theta(e^{\kappa t}-1). \]

We insert \(m(0)=x\).

\[ m(t)=xe^{-\kappa t}+\theta(1-e^{-\kappa t}). \]

We divide by \(e^{\kappa t}\). The mean moves from the initial value \(x\) toward the long-run level \(\theta\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(c) SDE for \(Y_t=X_t^2\)

\[ f(x)=x^2,\qquad f'(x)=2x,\qquad f''(x)=2. \]

We choose \(f\) because \(Y_t=f(X_t)\).

\[ dY_t=2X_t\,dX_t+\frac12\cdot2\,(dX_t)^2 =2X_t\,dX_t+(dX_t)^2. \]

This is Ito's formula specialized to \(x^2\).

\[ (dX_t)^2=\left(\sigma\sqrt{X_t(1-X_t)}\,dW_t\right)^2 =\sigma^2X_t(1-X_t)\,dt. \]

Only the Brownian part contributes to the square because \((dW_t)^2=dt\).

\[ 2X_t\,dX_t =2X_t\kappa(\theta-X_t)\,dt +2X_t\sigma\sqrt{X_t(1-X_t)}\,dW_t. \]

We multiply the original SDE by \(2X_t\).

\[ dY_t= 2\kappa\theta X_t\,dt-2\kappa X_t^2\,dt +2\sigma X_t\sqrt{X_t(1-X_t)}\,dW_t +\sigma^2X_t(1-X_t)\,dt. \]

We put the two parts of Ito's formula together.

\[ dY_t= \bigl((2\kappa\theta+\sigma^2)X_t-(2\kappa+\sigma^2)X_t^2\bigr)\,dt +2\sigma X_t\sqrt{X_t(1-X_t)}\,dW_t. \]

We collect the \(X_t\) terms and the \(X_t^2\) terms in the drift.

PDF check. This matches the SDE printed in the PDF.

(d) Differential equation for \(s(t)=E[X_t^2]\)

\[ s(t)=E[X_t^2]=E[Y_t]. \]

We use the process \(Y_t\) from part (c) because it is exactly \(X_t^2\).

\[ E[dY_t] = E\left[\bigl((2\kappa\theta+\sigma^2)X_t-(2\kappa+\sigma^2)X_t^2\bigr)\,dt\right]. \]

The Brownian integral part has expectation zero, so only the drift contributes to the derivative of the mean.

\[ s'(t)=(2\kappa\theta+\sigma^2)E[X_t]-(2\kappa+\sigma^2)E[X_t^2]. \]

We use linearity of expectation.

\[ s'(t)=(2\kappa\theta+\sigma^2)m(t)-(2\kappa+\sigma^2)s(t). \]

We replace \(E[X_t]\) by \(m(t)\) and \(E[X_t^2]\) by \(s(t)\).

Common mistakes. In Ito's formula, beginners often forget the \((dX_t)^2\) term. That term is essential because Brownian motion has quadratic variation.

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

Question 3

Complete question. The safe rate is zero. A risky asset \(S_t\) satisfies under \(P\)

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t}=\alpha X_t^2\,dt+X_t\,dW_t,\qquad S_0=s, \]

and \(X_t\) satisfies

\[ dX_t=-\lambda X_t\,dt+dW_t,\qquad X_0=x. \]

The same Brownian motion \(W_t\) appears in both equations.

  1. For \(dQ^h/dP=\exp(\int_0^T h_t\,dW_t-\frac12\int_0^T h_t^2\,dt)\), use Girsanov's theorem to write the dynamics under \(Q^h\).
  2. Find the risk-neutral \(h\), assuming \(dQ/dP\) is a martingale, and write the dynamics under \(Q\).
  3. Find the price of a derivative paying \(X_T\) at time \(T\).
  4. Find the price of a derivative paying \(X_TS_T\) at time \(T\).

Topic and purpose. This question tests Girsanov's theorem, risk-neutral pricing, and a change of measure using the stock price as a density.

Rules used. If \[ \frac{dQ^h}{dP}=\exp\left(\int_0^T h_t\,dW_t-\frac12\int_0^T h_t^2\,dt\right), \] then \[ \widetilde W_t^h=W_t-\int_0^t h_u\,du \] is Brownian motion under \(Q^h\). Equivalently, \(dW_t=d\widetilde W_t^h+h_tdt\).

(a) Dynamics under \(Q^h\)

\[ dW_t=d\widetilde W_t^h+h_t\,dt. \]

This is the useful substitution from Girsanov's theorem.

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t} =\alpha X_t^2dt+X_t(d\widetilde W_t^h+h_tdt). \]

We replace the old Brownian increment by the new Brownian increment plus the drift adjustment.

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t} =(\alpha X_t^2+h_tX_t)\,dt+X_t\,d\widetilde W_t^h =(\alpha X_t+h_t)X_t\,dt+X_t\,d\widetilde W_t^h. \]

We collect the \(dt\)-terms.

\[ dX_t=-\lambda X_tdt+d\widetilde W_t^h+h_tdt =(-\lambda X_t+h_t)dt+d\widetilde W_t^h. \]

We make the same Brownian substitution in the \(X_t\) equation.

PDF check. These dynamics match the PDF answer.

(b) Risk-neutral measure

\[ \text{safe rate}=0. \]

Under a risk-neutral measure, the drift of the risky asset must equal the safe rate. Since the safe rate is zero, the drift of \(S_t/S_t\) must be \(0\).

\[ (\alpha X_t+h_t)X_t=0. \]

This is the drift of \(dS_t/S_t\) under \(Q^h\).

\[ h_t=-\alpha X_t. \]

This choice makes the drift zero for all possible values of \(X_t\).

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t}=X_t\,d\widetilde W_t. \]

Substituting \(h_t=-\alpha X_t\) removes the drift of the asset price.

\[ dX_t=(-\lambda X_t-\alpha X_t)\,dt+d\widetilde W_t =-(\lambda+\alpha)X_t\,dt+d\widetilde W_t. \]

The \(X_t\) process becomes an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process under \(Q\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(c) Price of payoff \(X_T\)

\[ \beta=\lambda+\alpha. \]

We name the mean-reversion speed under \(Q\) to keep the notation short.

\[ dX_t=-\beta X_t\,dt+d\widetilde W_t. \]

This is a linear SDE.

\[ X_T=xe^{-\beta T}+\int_0^T e^{-\beta(T-u)}\,d\widetilde W_u. \]

This is the standard explicit solution of an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck SDE.

\[ E_Q[X_T]=xe^{-\beta T}+E_Q\left[\int_0^T e^{-\beta(T-u)}\,d\widetilde W_u\right] =xe^{-\beta T}. \]

The stochastic integral has mean zero.

\[ \text{price}=E_Q[X_T]=xe^{-(\lambda+\alpha)T}. \]

The safe rate is zero, so there is no discount factor.

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(d) Price of payoff \(X_TS_T\)

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t}=X_t\,d\widetilde W_t. \]

Under \(Q\), the stock has zero drift.

\[ S_T=s\exp\left(\int_0^T X_t\,d\widetilde W_t-\frac12\int_0^T X_t^2\,dt\right). \]

This is the stochastic exponential solution of \(dS_t/S_t=X_t\,d\widetilde W_t\).

\[ \text{price}=E_Q[S_TX_T] =s\,E_Q\left[ X_T\exp\left(\int_0^T X_t\,d\widetilde W_t-\frac12\int_0^T X_t^2\,dt\right) \right]. \]

We pull out the initial price \(s\).

\[ \frac{dR}{dQ} = \exp\left(\int_0^T X_t\,d\widetilde W_t-\frac12\int_0^T X_t^2\,dt\right). \]

We define a new measure \(R\). The density is exactly the exponential factor above.

\[ \widetilde W_t^R=\widetilde W_t-\int_0^t X_u\,du \]

By Girsanov's theorem, this is Brownian motion under \(R\).

\[ d\widetilde W_t=d\widetilde W_t^R+X_t\,dt. \]

We solve the previous equation for the old Brownian increment.

\[ dX_t=-\beta X_tdt+d\widetilde W_t^R+X_tdt =-(\beta-1)X_tdt+d\widetilde W_t^R. \]

The new measure changes the drift of \(X_t\) by \(+X_tdt\).

\[ E_R[X_T]=xe^{-(\beta-1)T}=xe^{-(\lambda+\alpha-1)T}. \]

The expectation of the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck solution is its initial value times the exponential drift factor.

\[ \text{price}=sE_R[X_T]=xs\,e^{-(\lambda+\alpha-1)T}. \]

The change of measure turns the hard expectation under \(Q\) into a simple expectation under \(R\).

Careful note. The PDF prints the final price \(xs e^{-(\lambda+\alpha-1)T}\), which is correct. One intermediate line in the PDF appears to write the drift as \(-(\lambda+\alpha+1)X_t\); the calculation above shows it should be \(-(\lambda+\alpha-1)X_t\).

Common mistakes. The most common error is using the wrong sign in Girsanov's theorem. Always write the Brownian relation first, then substitute it into the SDE.

PDF check. The final price matches the PDF answer.

Question 1

Complete question. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent random variables with \(X\sim\Gamma(2,\lambda)\), \(Y\sim\Gamma(3,\lambda)\), where \(\lambda\gt0\).

  1. Find the probability density function of \(S=X+Y\).
  2. Set \(R=X/S\). Show that \(R\) and \(S\) are independent and find the distribution of \(R\).
  3. Compute \(E[X^2Y]\).
  4. Compute \(M_D(t)=E[e^{t(X-Y)}]\) and state the range of \(t\) for which it exists.

Topic and purpose. This question tests the gamma distribution, the beta-gamma relationship, independence after a transformation, moments, and moment generating functions.

Rules used. For \(U\sim\Gamma(k,\lambda)\) with shape \(k\) and rate \(\lambda\), \[ f_U(u)=\frac{\lambda^k}{\Gamma(k)}u^{k-1}e^{-\lambda u},\qquad u\gt0. \] If two independent gamma variables have the same rate, their sum is gamma and the shapes add. Also, the moment generating function is \[ M_U(t)=E[e^{tU}]=\left(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-k},\qquad t\lt\lambda. \]

(a) Density of \(S=X+Y\)

\[ X\sim\Gamma(2,\lambda),\qquad Y\sim\Gamma(3,\lambda). \]

The shapes are \(2\) and \(3\), and the rates are the same.

\[ S=X+Y\sim\Gamma(2+3,\lambda)=\Gamma(5,\lambda). \]

For independent gamma variables with the same rate, the shapes add.

\[ f_S(s)=\frac{\lambda^5}{\Gamma(5)}s^{5-1}e^{-\lambda s},\qquad s\gt0. \]

We plug shape \(5\) into the gamma density.

\[ \Gamma(5)=4!=24, \qquad f_S(s)=\frac{\lambda^5}{24}s^4e^{-\lambda s},\qquad s\gt0. \]

For positive integers, \(\Gamma(n)=(n-1)!\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(b) Distribution of \(R=X/S\) and independence of \(R,S\)

\[ r=\frac{x}{x+y},\qquad s=x+y. \]

We transform from the original variables \((x,y)\) to the ratio and the sum \((r,s)\).

\[ x=rs,\qquad y=(1-r)s, \qquad 0\lt r\lt1,\ s\gt0. \]

These are the inverse transformation formulas.

\[ J= \left| \begin{matrix} \partial x/\partial r & \partial x/\partial s\\ \partial y/\partial r & \partial y/\partial s \end{matrix} \right| = \left| \begin{matrix} s & r\\ -s & 1-r \end{matrix} \right| =s(1-r)+rs=s. \]

The absolute value of the Jacobian tells us how area changes under the transformation.

\[ f_X(x)=\lambda^2xe^{-\lambda x}, \qquad f_Y(y)=\frac{\lambda^3}{2}y^2e^{-\lambda y}. \]

These are the gamma densities for shapes \(2\) and \(3\).

\[ f_{R,S}(r,s)=f_X(rs)f_Y((1-r)s)\cdot s. \]

The joint density after transformation is old joint density times the Jacobian.

\[ f_{R,S}(r,s)= \lambda^2(rs)e^{-\lambda rs} \cdot \frac{\lambda^3}{2}((1-r)s)^2e^{-\lambda(1-r)s} \cdot s. \]

We substitute \(x=rs\) and \(y=(1-r)s\).

\[ f_{R,S}(r,s)= \frac{\lambda^5}{2}r(1-r)^2s^4e^{-\lambda s}. \]

We combine \(e^{-\lambda rs}e^{-\lambda(1-r)s}=e^{-\lambda s}\), and the powers of \(s\) give \(s^4\).

\[ f_{R,S}(r,s)= \underbrace{12r(1-r)^2}_{f_R(r)} \underbrace{\frac{\lambda^5}{24}s^4e^{-\lambda s}}_{f_S(s)}. \]

The joint density factors into a function of \(r\) times a function of \(s\). That proves \(R\) and \(S\) are independent.

\[ R\sim\operatorname{Beta}(2,3), \qquad f_R(r)=\frac{\Gamma(5)}{\Gamma(2)\Gamma(3)}r^{1}(1-r)^2 =12r(1-r)^2. \]

The beta density has the form \(r^{a-1}(1-r)^{b-1}\) times a normalizing constant. Here \(a=2\), \(b=3\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer \(R\sim\operatorname{Beta}(2,3)\) and independence of \(R,S\).

(c) Compute \(E[X^2Y]\)

\[ E[X^2Y]=E[X^2]E[Y]. \]

We can multiply the expectations because \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent.

\[ E[X^2]=\frac{\Gamma(2+2)}{\Gamma(2)\lambda^2} =\frac{\Gamma(4)}{\Gamma(2)\lambda^2}. \]

This is the gamma moment formula \(E[X^m]=\Gamma(k+m)/(\Gamma(k)\lambda^m)\).

\[ \Gamma(4)=3!=6,\qquad \Gamma(2)=1!=1, \qquad E[X^2]=\frac{6}{\lambda^2}. \]

We evaluate the gamma functions.

\[ E[Y]=\frac{3}{\lambda}. \]

A gamma variable with shape \(k\) and rate \(\lambda\) has mean \(k/\lambda\). Here \(k=3\).

\[ E[X^2Y]=\frac{6}{\lambda^2}\cdot\frac{3}{\lambda} =\frac{18}{\lambda^3}. \]

We multiply the two factors and combine the powers of \(\lambda\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(d) Moment generating function of \(D=X-Y\)

\[ M_D(t)=E[e^{t(X-Y)}]=E[e^{tX}e^{-tY}]. \]

We split the exponential using \(e^{a-b}=e^ae^{-b}\).

\[ M_D(t)=E[e^{tX}]E[e^{-tY}]. \]

Independence lets us multiply the expectations.

\[ E[e^{tX}]=\left(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-2}, \qquad t\lt\lambda. \]

We use the gamma MGF with shape \(2\).

\[ E[e^{-tY}]=\left(1+\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-3}, \qquad -t\lt\lambda. \]

We substitute \(-t\) into the gamma MGF for \(Y\), whose shape is \(3\).

\[ M_D(t)= \left(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-2} \left(1+\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-3}. \]

We multiply the two MGF factors.

\[ t\lt\lambda \quad\text{and}\quad -t\lt\lambda \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad -\lambda\lt t\lt\lambda. \]

Both expectations must exist, so both restrictions must hold.

Common mistakes. Do not use the gamma addition rule when rates are different; it works here because both rates are \(\lambda\). Also remember that \(E[e^{-tY}]\) changes the sign in the MGF.

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

Question 2

Complete question. Let \(W_t\) be Brownian motion and define

Topic and purpose. This question tests Brownian martingales, stochastic differentials, Ito isometry, and Girsanov's theorem.

(a) Martingale property and expectation

\[ E[X_t\mid\mathcal F_s] =E\left[tW_t-\int_0^t W_r\,dr\ \middle|\ \mathcal F_s\right], \qquad 0\le s\lt t. \]

To prove the martingale property, we check whether the conditional expectation at time \(s\) equals \(X_s\).

\[ E[W_t\mid\mathcal F_s]=W_s. \]

Brownian increments after time \(s\) are independent of \(\mathcal F_s\) and have mean zero.

\[ \int_0^t W_r\,dr=\int_0^s W_r\,dr+\int_s^t W_r\,dr. \]

We split the integral into the part already known at time \(s\) and the future part.

\[ E\left[\int_0^s W_r\,dr\ \middle|\ \mathcal F_s\right] =\int_0^s W_r\,dr. \]

The first integral is already known at time \(s\).

\[ E\left[\int_s^t W_r\,dr\ \middle|\ \mathcal F_s\right] =\int_s^t E[W_r\mid\mathcal F_s]\,dr =\int_s^t W_s\,dr =W_s(t-s). \]

For every future \(r\), the best prediction of \(W_r\) at time \(s\) is \(W_s\).

\[ E[X_t\mid\mathcal F_s] =tW_s-\left(\int_0^s W_r\,dr+W_s(t-s)\right) =sW_s-\int_0^s W_r\,dr =X_s. \]

The \(tW_s-W_s(t-s)\) part simplifies to \(sW_s\), giving exactly \(X_s\).

\[ E[X_t]=E[X_0]=0. \]

A martingale has constant expectation, and \(X_0=0\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(b) Differential form

\[ d(tW_t)=W_t\,dt+t\,dW_t. \]

This is the product rule: \(t\) changes by \(dt\), and \(W_t\) changes by \(dW_t\).

\[ d\left(\int_0^t W_s\,ds\right)=W_t\,dt. \]

The derivative of an accumulated integral is the current integrand.

\[ dX_t=d(tW_t)-d\left(\int_0^t W_s\,ds\right) =(W_tdt+tdW_t)-W_tdt =t\,dW_t. \]

The \(W_tdt\) terms cancel.

\[ b_t=0,\qquad a_t=t. \]

Comparing \(dX_t=t\,dW_t\) with \(dX_t=b_tdt+a_tdW_t\), the drift is \(0\) and the diffusion coefficient is \(t\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(c) Variance of \(X_t\)

\[ X_t=\int_0^t s\,dW_s. \]

This follows from part (b) and \(X_0=0\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X_t)=E[X_t^2]-(E[X_t])^2=E[X_t^2]. \]

From part (a), \(E[X_t]=0\).

\[ E[X_t^2] =E\left[\left(\int_0^t s\,dW_s\right)^2\right] =\int_0^t s^2\,ds. \]

This is Ito isometry: the second moment of a stochastic integral equals the integral of the square of the integrand.

\[ \int_0^t s^2\,ds=\left[\frac{s^3}{3}\right]_0^t=\frac{t^3}{3}. \]

We integrate the ordinary function \(s^2\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X_t)=\frac{t^3}{3}. \]

This is the required variance.

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

\[ \frac{dQ}{dP}\Big|_{\mathcal F_t} = \exp\left(\int_0^t W_s\,dW_s-\frac12\int_0^t W_s^2\,ds\right). \]

\[ \widetilde W_t=W_t-\int_0^t W_s\,ds \]

By Girsanov's theorem, this is Brownian motion under \(Q\).

\[ dW_t=d\widetilde W_t+W_t\,dt. \]

We rearrange the Brownian relation so it can be substituted into \(dX_t\).

\[ dX_t=t\,dW_t=t(d\widetilde W_t+W_tdt) =tW_tdt+t\,d\widetilde W_t. \]

The old Brownian increment gains a drift term under the new measure.

\[ \widetilde b_t=tW_t,\qquad \widetilde a_t=t. \]

We compare with \(dX_t=\widetilde b_tdt+\widetilde a_td\widetilde W_t\).

We rearrange the Girsanov relation to get the dynamics of the original \(W_t\) under \(Q\).

\[ \boxed{dW_t=-\frac{W_t}{T-t}dt+d\widetilde W_t,\qquad t\lt T.} \]

This is Brownian motion pulled toward \(0\) as \(t\) approaches \(T\); it is the Brownian bridge drift.

Technical note. The formula is rigorous for every \(t\lt T\). At \(T\), the density becomes singular; intuitively the new measure conditions the Brownian path to end at \(0\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF dynamics for \(t\lt T\).

(d) Generalization to endpoint \(a\)

\[ f^{(a)}(t,w)=\sqrt{\frac{T}{T-t}} \exp\left(-\frac{(a-w)^2}{2(T-t)}+\frac{a^2}{2T}\right). \]

This is the same type of function, but it pulls the Brownian path toward endpoint \(a\) instead of \(0\).

\[ f_w^{(a)}(t,w)=\frac{a-w}{T-t}f^{(a)}(t,w). \]

The derivative of \(-(a-w)^2/[2(T-t)]\) with respect to \(w\) is \((a-w)/(T-t)\).

\[ f_t^{(a)}+\frac12f_{ww}^{(a)}=0. \]

The same cancellation as in part (a) occurs. The added constant \(a^2/(2T)\) has zero derivative in \(t\) and \(w\).

\[ dZ_t(a)=\frac{a-W_t}{T-t}Z_t(a)\,dW_t. \]

Ito's formula leaves only the Brownian term.

\[ E[Z_t(a)]=1. \]

Integrating against the normal density of \(W_t\), completing the square gives cancellation just like in part (b). Therefore \(Z_t(a)\) is a martingale for \(t\lt T\).

\[ \theta_t^{(a)}=\frac{a-W_t}{T-t}. \]

This is the coefficient in the stochastic exponential.

\[ \widetilde W_t^{(a)} = W_t-\int_0^t \frac{a-W_s}{T-s}\,ds \]

By Girsanov's theorem, this is Brownian motion under the new measure \(Q^{(a)}\).

\[ dW_t=\frac{a-W_t}{T-t}dt+d\widetilde W_t^{(a)},\qquad t\lt T. \]

The drift pulls \(W_t\) toward the endpoint \(a\).

Common mistakes. Do not ignore the time derivative of the prefactor \(\sqrt{T/(T-t)}\). Without it, the drift cancellation is missed.

Question 3

Complete question. An asset price \(S_t\) satisfies

\[ dS_t=\kappa S_t(\bar S-S_t)\,dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t,\qquad S_0\gt0, \]

where \(\kappa,\bar S,\sigma\gt0\).

  1. Define \(X_t=1/S_t\) and derive the SDE satisfied by \(X_t\).
  2. Using Ito's formula, show that \[ X_t=e^{(\frac12\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S)t-\sigma W_t} \left( X_0+\kappa\int_0^t e^{-(\frac12\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S)s+\sigma W_s}\,ds \right). \]
  3. Deduce an explicit expression for \(S_t\).
  4. For horizon \(T\gt0\) and \(\theta\in\mathbb R\), define \(Q\) on \(\mathcal F_T\) by \[ \frac{dQ}{dP}\Big|_{\mathcal F_T}=\exp\left(-\theta W_T-\frac12\theta^2T\right). \] Let \(W_t^Q=W_t+\theta t\). Write the dynamics of \(S_t\) under \(Q\), and give the explicit formula for \(S_t\) under \(Q\), for \(t\le T\).

Topic and purpose. This question tests Ito's formula, reciprocal transformations, solving linear SDEs with an integrating factor, and changing Brownian drift under Girsanov.

(a) SDE for \(X_t=1/S_t\)

\[ f(s)=\frac1s=s^{-1},\qquad f'(s)=-s^{-2},\qquad f''(s)=2s^{-3}. \]

We need these derivatives because \(X_t=f(S_t)\).

\[ dX_t=f'(S_t)dS_t+\frac12f''(S_t)(dS_t)^2 =-\frac{1}{S_t^2}dS_t+\frac{1}{S_t^3}(dS_t)^2. \]

This is Ito's formula.

\[ (dS_t)^2=(\sigma S_t\,dW_t)^2=\sigma^2S_t^2\,dt. \]

Only the Brownian part contributes to the square.

\[ -\frac{1}{S_t^2}dS_t = -\frac{1}{S_t^2} \left(\kappa S_t(\bar S-S_t)dt+\sigma S_t\,dW_t\right). \]

We substitute the given SDE for \(dS_t\).

\[ -\frac{1}{S_t^2}dS_t = -\kappa\frac{\bar S-S_t}{S_t}dt-\sigma\frac1{S_t}dW_t. \]

We cancel one power of \(S_t\).

\[ \frac{1}{S_t^3}(dS_t)^2 = \frac{1}{S_t^3}\sigma^2S_t^2dt = \sigma^2\frac1{S_t}dt. \]

We simplify the second-order Ito term.

\[ \frac{\bar S-S_t}{S_t}=\bar S\frac1{S_t}-1=\bar S X_t-1. \]

We rewrite everything in terms of \(X_t=1/S_t\).

\[ dX_t = -\kappa(\bar S X_t-1)dt-\sigma X_t\,dW_t+\sigma^2X_tdt. \]

We combine the pieces.

\[ dX_t=\left(\kappa+(\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S)X_t\right)dt-\sigma X_t\,dW_t, \qquad X_0=\frac1{S_0}. \]

We collect the constant drift term and the coefficient of \(X_t\).

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(b) Verify the explicit formula for \(X_t\)

\[ A=\frac12\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S. \]

We name this constant because it appears repeatedly.

\[ Y_t=e^{At-\sigma W_t}, \qquad M_t=X_0+\kappa\int_0^t e^{-As+\sigma W_s}\,ds. \]

The proposed solution is \(X_t=Y_tM_t\).

\[ dY_t=Y_t\left(A\,dt-\sigma\,dW_t+\frac12\sigma^2dt\right) =Y_t\left((A+\frac12\sigma^2)dt-\sigma dW_t\right). \]

Ito's formula for an exponential includes the extra \(\frac12\sigma^2dt\) term.

\[ A+\frac12\sigma^2 = \left(\frac12\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S\right)+\frac12\sigma^2 = \sigma^2-\kappa\bar S. \]

This shows why \(A\) was chosen.

\[ dM_t=\kappa e^{-At+\sigma W_t}dt=\frac{\kappa}{Y_t}dt. \]

The derivative of the time integral is the integrand at time \(t\).

\[ dX_t=d(Y_tM_t)=Y_tdM_t+M_tdY_t+d\langle Y,M\rangle_t. \]

This is the product rule for semimartingales.

\[ d\langle Y,M\rangle_t=0. \]

\(M_t\) has only a \(dt\)-part and no \(dW_t\)-part, so it has no quadratic covariation with \(Y_t\).

\[ Y_tdM_t=\kappa dt. \]

Since \(dM_t=\kappa/Y_t\,dt\), multiplying by \(Y_t\) leaves \(\kappa dt\).

\[ M_tdY_t=Y_tM_t\left((\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S)dt-\sigma dW_t\right) =X_t\left((\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S)dt-\sigma dW_t\right). \]

We use \(X_t=Y_tM_t\).

\[ dX_t=\kappa dt+(\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S)X_tdt-\sigma X_tdW_t. \]

This is exactly the SDE from part (a), so the proposed formula is verified.

PDF check. This matches the PDF answer.

(c) Explicit expression for \(S_t\)

\[ S_t=\frac1{X_t}. \]

By definition, \(X_t=1/S_t\).

\[ X_t=e^{At-\sigma W_t} \left(X_0+\kappa\int_0^t e^{-As+\sigma W_s}\,ds\right). \]

We start from the formula proved in part (b).

\[ S_t= \frac{e^{-At+\sigma W_t}} {X_0+\kappa\int_0^t e^{-As+\sigma W_s}\,ds}. \]

Taking the reciprocal inverts the exponential factor.

\[ -A=\kappa\bar S-\frac12\sigma^2, \qquad X_0=\frac1{S_0}. \]

We rewrite the constants in the original model notation.

\[ \boxed{ S_t= \frac{e^{(\kappa\bar S-\frac12\sigma^2)t+\sigma W_t}} {\frac1{S_0}+\kappa\int_0^t e^{(\kappa\bar S-\frac12\sigma^2)s+\sigma W_s}\,ds}. } \]

This is the explicit expression for \(S_t\).

(d) Dynamics and explicit formula under \(Q\)

\[ \frac{dQ}{dP}\Big|_{\mathcal F_T} = \exp\left(-\theta W_T-\frac12\theta^2T\right), \qquad W_t^Q=W_t+\theta t. \]

By Girsanov's theorem, \(W^Q\) is Brownian motion under \(Q\) on \([0,T]\).

\[ dW_t=dW_t^Q-\theta dt. \]

We rearrange \(W_t^Q=W_t+\theta t\).

\[ dS_t=\kappa S_t(\bar S-S_t)dt+\sigma S_t(dW_t^Q-\theta dt). \]

We substitute the \(Q\)-Brownian increment into the original SDE.

\[ dS_t= \left(\kappa S_t(\bar S-S_t)-\sigma\theta S_t\right)dt +\sigma S_t\,dW_t^Q. \]

The measure change subtracts \(\sigma\theta S_t\) from the drift.

\[ dX_t= \left(\kappa+(\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S+\sigma\theta)X_t\right)dt -\sigma X_t\,dW_t^Q. \]

This follows from the reciprocal SDE in part (a) and \(dW_t=dW_t^Q-\theta dt\).

\[ A_\theta=\frac12\sigma^2-\kappa\bar S+\sigma\theta. \]

This is the new integrating-factor constant under \(Q\).

\[ X_t= e^{A_\theta t-\sigma W_t^Q} \left( X_0+\kappa\int_0^t e^{-A_\theta s+\sigma W_s^Q}\,ds \right), \qquad t\le T. \]

This is the same linear-SDE solution as before, with \(W\) replaced by \(W^Q\) and \(A\) replaced by \(A_\theta\).

\[ S_t= \frac{e^{-A_\theta t+\sigma W_t^Q}} {X_0+\kappa\int_0^t e^{-A_\theta s+\sigma W_s^Q}\,ds}, \qquad t\le T. \]

We take the reciprocal to get \(S_t\).

Common mistakes. The sign of \(\theta\) is easy to reverse. Start from \(W_t^Q=W_t+\theta t\), then solve for \(dW_t\).

Common Exam Mistakes

  • Forgetting one branch of an inverse transformation, especially \(x=\pm\sqrt y\).
  • Forgetting the absolute value in a Jacobian or derivative.
  • Using a gamma sum rule when the rates are not the same.
  • Confusing rate \(\lambda\) with scale \(1/\lambda\).
  • Dropping the \(\frac12 f''(X_t)(dX_t)^2\) term in Ito's formula.
  • Using \(dW_t^2=0\) instead of the correct rule \((dW_t)^2=dt\).
  • Getting the sign wrong in Girsanov. Write the Brownian relation first.
  • Pricing under the physical measure \(P\) instead of the risk-neutral measure \(Q\).
  • Confusing \(\max(R_T,K)\) with \((R_T-K)^+\).
  • Forgetting the range of \(t\) where an MGF exists.

One-Page Revision Sheet

Random variable transformations. Solve \(y=g(x)\), list every root \(x_i\), compute \(|g'(x_i)|\), then add \(f_X(x_i)/|g'(x_i)|\).

Gamma and beta. Same-rate gamma variables add. If \(X\sim\Gamma(a,\lambda)\) and \(Y\sim\Gamma(b,\lambda)\), then \(X/(X+Y)\sim\operatorname{Beta}(a,b)\) and is independent of \(X+Y\).

Conditioning. Use \(E[X]=E[E[X\mid N]]\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[\operatorname{Var}(X\mid N)]+\operatorname{Var}(E[X\mid N])\).

Ito checklist. Compute \(f_t\), \(f_x\), \(f_{xx}\). Replace \((dW)^2\) by \(dt\). Remove \(dt\,dW\) and \((dt)^2\).

Girsanov checklist. Identify \(\theta_t\) from the density \(dZ_t=Z_t\theta_t\,dW_t\). Then \(\widetilde W_t=W_t-\int_0^t\theta_sds\) is Brownian under the new measure.

Pricing. Under \(Q\), discounted asset prices are martingales. Derivative price \(=e^{-rT}E_Q[\text{payoff}]\).

Tips for Similar Exams

  1. Write down the distribution or SDE exactly before starting.
  2. Name complicated expressions, such as \(A=\sqrt pX_1+\sqrt{1-p}X_2\) or \(\beta=\lambda+\alpha\).
  3. For density transformations, draw a small domain note: positive, negative, interval, or all real numbers.
  4. For SDE questions, separate drift terms from Brownian terms before simplifying.
  5. For Girsanov questions, decide the sign convention once and stick to it all the way through.
  6. At the end, compare dimensions and behavior. For example, variances must be nonnegative and densities must integrate to one.
