Basic Probability and Moments

Density Normalization

Formula:

\[\int_{\text{support}} f_X(x)\,dx=1,\qquad f_X(x)\ge0\]

Description:

A continuous density must have total area one and cannot be negative.

Purpose:

Use it to check a density or find an unknown normalizing constant.

When to Use:

Use when a question gives a proposed density or asks whether a density is valid.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: density, pdf, normalize, find the constant, valid probability density.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(f_X(x)\) is the density of \(X\). The support is the set of values where \(X\) can lie.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The variable must be continuous, and the integral is only over the support.

Related Formulas:

CDF and density relationship; expectation integral.

Common Mistakes:

Integrating over the wrong range or forgetting nonnegativity.

CDF and Density RelationshipHigh priority

Formula:

\[F_X(x)=P(X\le x),\qquad f_X(x)=F_X'(x)\]

Description:

The CDF gives probability up to \(x\); differentiating it gives the density.

Purpose:

Use it to move between CDFs and densities.

When to Use:

Use after finding a probability such as \(P(Y\le y)\).

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: distribution function, cumulative distribution, find the density, CDF method.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(F_X\) is the CDF, \(f_X\) is the density, and \(x\) is a real value.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The derivative formula applies where the CDF is differentiable.

Related Formulas:

Uniform area rule; change of variables.

Common Mistakes:

Differentiating outside the support or forgetting piecewise cases.

Expectation IntegralHigh priority

Formula:

\[E[g(X)]=\int_{\text{support}} g(x)f_X(x)\,dx\]

Description:

This gives the average value of a function of a continuous random variable.

Purpose:

Use it to calculate means, moments, and expected payoffs.

When to Use:

Use when the density is known and an expectation is requested.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: expected value, mean, moment, payoff, average.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(g\) is a function of \(X\). \(f_X\) is the density of \(X\).

Conditions or Assumptions:

The integral must exist as a finite number.

Related Formulas:

Variance identity; risk-neutral pricing; MGF.

Common Mistakes:

Using \(g(E[X])\) instead of \(E[g(X)]\).

Variance IdentityHigh priority

Formula:

\[\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2\]

Description:

Variance measures spread around the mean.

Purpose:

Use it once the first and second moments are known.

When to Use:

Use when asked for variance and you can compute moments.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: variance, spread, second moment, standard deviation.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(E[X]\) is the mean and \(E[X^2]\) is the second moment.

Conditions or Assumptions:

Requires finite second moment.

Related Formulas:

Law of total variance; Ito isometry.

Common Mistakes:

Forgetting the square on \((E[X])^2\).

Covariance Identity

Formula:

\[\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]\]

Description:

Covariance measures how two random variables move together.

Purpose:

Use it to calculate dependence and cross terms.

When to Use:

Use when covariance or correlation appears.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: covariance, correlation, joint movement, cross term.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(X,Y\) are random variables; \(E[XY]\) is their product moment.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The expectations must be finite.

Related Formulas:

Quadratic covariation; covariance by conditioning.

Common Mistakes:

Assuming covariance is zero without independence or another reason.

Moment Generating FunctionHigh priority

Formula:

\[M_X(t)=E[e^{tX}],\qquad E[X^n]=M_X^{(n)}(0)\]

Description:

The MGF packages all moments into one function when it exists near zero.

Purpose:

Use it to find moments or identify a distribution.

When to Use:

Use when asked for an MGF or when moments are easiest by differentiating.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: MGF, moment generating, identify distribution.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(t\) is the MGF variable; \(M_X^{(n)}(0)\) is the \(n\)-th derivative at zero.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The MGF must be finite around \(t=0\).

Related Formulas:

Normal MGF; gamma MGF.

Common Mistakes:

Ignoring the allowed range of \(t\).

Formula:

\[(X,Y)\sim U([0,1]^2)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad P((X,Y)\in A)=\operatorname{Area}(A)\]

Description:

For a uniform point in the unit square, probability equals geometric area.

Purpose:

Use it for inequalities involving two independent uniform variables.

When to Use:

Use when the event is a region in the square.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: independent uniform, unit square, area, region, ratio of uniforms.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(A\) is a region inside \([0,1]^2\).

Conditions or Assumptions:

The joint density must be constant on the square.

Related Formulas:

CDF method; ratio distribution.

Common Mistakes:

Drawing the wrong region or missing separate cases.

One-to-One Density TransformationHigh priority

Formula:

\[f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\left|\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|\]

Description:

This transforms a density through an invertible function.

Purpose:

Use it to find the density of \(Y=g(X)\).

When to Use:

Use when the transformation has one inverse on the support.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: transformed variable, inverse, density of \(Y\).

Meaning of Symbols:

\(g^{-1}\) is the inverse transformation; the absolute derivative is the one-dimensional Jacobian.

Conditions or Assumptions:

\(g\) must be differentiable and one-to-one on the support.

Related Formulas:

Many-to-one transformation; CDF method.

Common Mistakes:

Forgetting the absolute value or using the derivative in the wrong direction.

Many-to-One Density TransformationHigh priority

Formula:

\[f_Y(y)=\sum_i \frac{f_X(x_i)}{|g'(x_i)|},\qquad g(x_i)=y\]

Description:

This handles transformations where several inputs produce the same output.

Purpose:

Use it for squares, absolute values, and other non-invertible transformations.

When to Use:

Use when \(g(x)=y\) has more than one root.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: square, absolute value, multiple roots, non-monotone transformation.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(x_i\) are all roots in the support; \(g'\) is the derivative of \(g\).

Conditions or Assumptions:

Include every valid root and avoid points where \(g'(x_i)=0\) unless handled carefully.

Related Formulas:

One-to-one transformation; CDF method.

Common Mistakes:

Forgetting the negative root for a square.

Two-Dimensional Jacobian FormulaHigh priority

Formula:

\[f_{U,V}(u,v)=f_{X,Y}(x(u,v),y(u,v))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\]

Description:

This changes variables in a joint density.

Purpose:

Use it for sums, ratios, and beta-gamma transformations.

When to Use:

Use when two old variables are replaced by two new variables.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: joint density, transformation, Jacobian, sum and ratio.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(x(u,v),y(u,v)\) are inverse transformations; the determinant is the Jacobian.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The transformation must be one-to-one on the relevant support.

Related Formulas:

Beta-gamma ratio independence; ratio density.

Common Mistakes:

Using the reciprocal Jacobian or forgetting support restrictions.

Ratio Density Formula

Formula:

\[Z=Y/X\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_Z(z)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|x|f_{X,Y}(x,zx)\,dx\]

Description:

This is a direct density formula for a ratio of random variables.

Purpose:

Use it when a ratio is requested and a joint density is known.

When to Use:

Use for \(Y/X\), \(X/Y\), quotient, or fraction distributions.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: ratio, quotient, divided by, fraction of random variables.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(f_{X,Y}\) is the joint density; \(|x|\) is the Jacobian factor.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The denominator must not be zero with positive probability.

Related Formulas:

CDF method; Jacobian formula.

Common Mistakes:

Forgetting the absolute Jacobian factor.

Normal and Lognormal Formulas

Normal DensityHigh priority

Formula:

\[X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}\]

Description:

This is the density of a normal random variable.

Purpose:

Use it for Gaussian calculations and Brownian fixed-time values.

When to Use:

Use whenever a variable is normal or Brownian motion is evaluated at a fixed time.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: normal, Gaussian, \(N(\mu,\sigma^2)\), Brownian at time \(t\).

Meaning of Symbols:

\(\mu\) is mean; \(\sigma^2\) is variance; \(\sigma\) is standard deviation.

Conditions or Assumptions:

\(\sigma\gt0\).

Related Formulas:

Standardization; normal exponential moment.

Common Mistakes:

Confusing variance and standard deviation.

Normal StandardizationHigh priority

Formula:

\[P(X\le x)=N\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right),\qquad X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\]

Description:

Standardization converts any normal variable to a standard normal variable.

Purpose:

Use it to compute normal and lognormal probabilities.

When to Use:

Use for events involving a normal or lognormal variable.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: probability, threshold, normal CDF, \(N(\cdot)\).

Meaning of Symbols:

\(N(\cdot)\) is the standard normal CDF.

Conditions or Assumptions:

\(\sigma\gt0\).

Related Formulas:

Lognormal option formulas; \(d_1,d_2\).

Common Mistakes:

Dividing by variance instead of standard deviation.

Normal Exponential MomentHigh priority

Formula:

\[W_t\sim N(0,t)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[e^{uW_t}]=e^{u^2t/2}\]

Description:

This gives the expectation of an exponential of Brownian motion.

Purpose:

Use it for exponential martingales and lognormal moments.

When to Use:

Use when the expectation contains \(e^{uW_t}\).

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: exponential of Brownian motion, lognormal, moment.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(u\) is a constant; \(W_t\) is Brownian motion at time \(t\).

Conditions or Assumptions:

The variable must be normal with mean 0 and variance \(t\).

Related Formulas:

Brownian exponential martingale; lognormal moment.

Common Mistakes:

Writing \(e^{ut/2}\) instead of \(e^{u^2t/2}\).

Normal Fourth Moment

Formula:

\[W_T\sim N(0,T)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[W_T^2]=T,\\quad E[W_T^4]=3T^2\]

Description:

These are standard Brownian fixed-time moments.

Use them for expectations involving \(W_T^2\) and \(W_T^4\).

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Brownian motion starts at 0 under the measure being used.

Related Formulas:

Gaussian integral; change of measure expectation.

Using \(T^2\) instead of \(3T^2\).

Lognormal MomentHigh priority

Formula:

\[Y=e^X,\ X\sim N(m,v)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[Y^q]=e^{qm+q^2v/2}\]

Description:

A lognormal variable is an exponential of a normal variable.

Purpose:

Use it for moments of geometric Brownian motion.

When to Use:

Use when \(\log Y\) is normal.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: lognormal, exponential Brownian, moment of \(e^X\).

Meaning of Symbols:

\(m\) is mean of \(X\), \(v\) is variance, \(q\) is a power.

Conditions or Assumptions:

\(X\) must be normally distributed.

Related Formulas:

GBM solution; normal exponential moment.

Common Mistakes:

Forgetting the variance correction \(q^2v/2\).

Gamma, Beta, and Discrete Distributions

Gamma DensityHigh priority

Formula:

\[X\sim\Gamma(k,\lambda)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\frac{\lambda^k}{\Gamma(k)}x^{k-1}e^{-\lambda x},\quad x\gt0\]

Description:

The gamma distribution has shape \(k\) and rate \(\lambda\).

Purpose:

Use it for gamma variables and gamma mixtures.

When to Use:

Use when the density looks like a power times an exponential.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: gamma, shape, rate, \(x^{k-1}e^{-\lambda x}\).

Meaning of Symbols:

\(k\gt0\) is shape; \(\lambda\gt0\) is rate.

Conditions or Assumptions:

Support is \(x\gt0\).

Related Formulas:

Gamma MGF; gamma sum; beta-gamma relationship.

Common Mistakes:

Confusing rate \(\lambda\) with scale \(1/\lambda\).

Gamma Mean, Variance, and MGFHigh priority

Formula:

\[E[X]=\frac{k}{\lambda},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{k}{\lambda^2},\qquad M_X(t)=\left(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-k}\]

Description:

These are the main summary formulas for a gamma variable.

Purpose:

Use them to calculate moments or identify gamma distributions.

When to Use:

Use when \(X\sim\Gamma(k,\lambda)\).

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Keywords: gamma mean, gamma variance, gamma MGF.

Meaning of Symbols:

\(k\) is shape, \(\lambda\) is rate, \(t\) is MGF variable.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The MGF needs \(t\lt\lambda\).

Related Formulas:

Gamma density; gamma moment formula.

Common Mistakes:

Writing variance as \(k/\lambda\) instead of \(k/\lambda^2\).

Gamma Moment Formula

Formula:

\[E[X^m]=\frac{\Gamma(k+m)}{\Gamma(k)\lambda^m},\qquad X\sim\Gamma(k,\lambda)\]

Description:

This gives higher moments of gamma variables.

Purpose:

Use it for \(E[X^2]\) and other powers.

When to Use:

Use when asked for powers of a gamma variable.

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Meaning of Symbols:

\(m\) is the moment order.

Conditions or Assumptions:

The moment must be finite; positive moments are finite.

Related Formulas:

Gamma recursion; gamma mean and variance.

Common Mistakes:

Forgetting \(\lambda^m\) in the denominator.

Beta IntegralHigh priority

Formula:

\[\int_0^1x^{u-1}(1-x)^{v-1}\,dx=\frac{\Gamma(u)\Gamma(v)}{\Gamma(u+v)}\]

Description:

Use it for beta moments and beta-type integrals.

Related Formulas:

Beta density; gamma recursion.

Beta DensityHigh priority

Formula:

\[X\sim\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1},\quad0\lt x\lt1\]

Description:

The beta distribution is a distribution on \((0,1)\).

Purpose:

Use it for proportions and gamma ratios.

When to Use:

Use when a variable is between 0 and 1 with powers of \(x\) and \(1-x\).

How to Recognize It in an Exam:

Meaning of Symbols:

\(\alpha,\beta\gt0\) are beta shape parameters.

Conditions or Assumptions:

Support is \(0\lt x\lt1\).

Related Formulas:

Beta mean and variance; beta-gamma relationship.

Common Mistakes:

Using it outside \([0,1]\).

Basic Probability and Moments

  • Density Normalization: \(\int_{\text{support}} f_X(x)\,dx=1,\qquad f_X(x)\ge0\)
  • CDF and Density Relationship: \(F_X(x)=P(X\le x),\qquad f_X(x)=F_X'(x)\)
  • Expectation Integral: \(E[g(X)]=\int_{\text{support}} g(x)f_X(x)\,dx\)
  • Variance Identity: \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2\)
  • Covariance Identity: \(\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[XY]-E[X]E[Y]\)
  • Moment Generating Function: \(M_X(t)=E[e^{tX}],\qquad E[X^n]=M_X^{(n)}(0)\)

Transformations and Change of Variables

  • Uniform Area Rule: \((X,Y)\sim U([0,1]^2)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad P((X,Y)\in A)=\operatorname{Area}(A)\)
  • One-to-One Density Transformation: \(f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\left|\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|\)
  • Many-to-One Density Transformation: \(f_Y(y)=\sum_i \frac{f_X(x_i)}{|g'(x_i)|},\qquad g(x_i)=y\)
  • Two-Dimensional Jacobian Formula: \(f_{U,V}(u,v)=f_{X,Y}(x(u,v),y(u,v))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\)
  • Ratio Density Formula: \(Z=Y/X\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_Z(z)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|x|f_{X,Y}(x,zx)\,dx\)

Normal and Lognormal Formulas

  • Normal Density: \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}\)
  • Normal Standardization: \(P(X\le x)=N\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right),\qquad X\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2)\)
  • Normal Exponential Moment: \(W_t\sim N(0,t)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[e^{uW_t}]=e^{u^2t/2}\)
  • Normal Fourth Moment: \(W_T\sim N(0,T)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[W_T^2]=T,\\quad E[W_T^4]=3T^2\)
  • Lognormal Moment: \(Y=e^X,\ X\sim N(m,v)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[Y^q]=e^{qm+q^2v/2}\)

Gamma, Beta, and Discrete Distributions

  • Gamma Density: \(X\sim\Gamma(k,\lambda)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\frac{\lambda^k}{\Gamma(k)}x^{k-1}e^{-\lambda x},\quad x\gt0\)
  • Gamma Mean, Variance, and MGF: \(E[X]=\frac{k}{\lambda},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{k}{\lambda^2},\qquad M_X(t)=\left(1-\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-k}\)
  • Gamma Moment Formula: \(E[X^m]=\frac{\Gamma(k+m)}{\Gamma(k)\lambda^m},\qquad X\sim\Gamma(k,\lambda)\)
  • Gamma Sum Rule: \(X\sim\Gamma(a,\lambda),\ Y\sim\Gamma(b,\lambda),\ X\perp Y\quad\Longrightarrow\quad X+Y\sim\Gamma(a+b,\lambda)\)
  • Gamma Function Recursion: \(\Gamma(q+1)=q\Gamma(q),\qquad \Gamma(n)=(n-1)!\)
  • Beta Integral: \(\int_0^1x^{u-1}(1-x)^{v-1}\,dx=\frac{\Gamma(u)\Gamma(v)}{\Gamma(u+v)}\)
  • Beta Density: \(X\sim\operatorname{Beta}(\alpha,\beta)\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1},\quad0\lt x\lt1\)
  • Beta Mean and Variance: \(E[X]=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta},\qquad \operatorname{Var}(X)=\frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)}\)
  • Beta-Gamma Relationship: \(X\sim\Gamma(a,\lambda),\ Y\sim\Gamma(b,\lambda),\ X\perp Y\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{X}{X+Y}\sim\operatorname{Beta}(a,b),\quad X+Y\sim\Gamma(a+b,\lambda)\)
  • Exponential Distribution: \(X\sim\operatorname{Exp}(\lambda)\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad f_X(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x},\ x\gt0,\qquad E[X]=1/\lambda\)
  • Geometric Distribution on 1,2,...: \(P(N=n)=p(1-p)^{n-1},\qquad E[N]=\frac1p,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(N)=\frac{1-p}{p^2}\)
  • Pascal Distribution on 0,1,...: \(P(N=n)=\binom{n+r-1}{n}(1-p)^rp^n,\quad E[N]=\frac{rp}{1-p},\quad \operatorname{Var}(N)=\frac{rp}{(1-p)^2}\)
  • Poisson Distribution: \(P(N=n)=e^{-m}\frac{m^n}{n!},\qquad E[N]=m,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(N)=m\)
  • Exponential Series: \(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{a^n}{n!}=e^a\)

Conditioning and Mixtures

  • Discrete-Continuous Joint Law: \(f_{N,X}(n,x)=P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x)\)
  • Marginal Mixture Density: \(f_X(x)=\sum_n P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x)\)
  • Bayes Formula for Mixed Variables: \(P(N=n|X=x)=\frac{P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x)}{f_X(x)}\)
  • Law of Total Expectation: \(E[X]=E[E[X|Y]]\)
  • Law of Total Variance: \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=E[\operatorname{Var}(X|Y)]+\operatorname{Var}(E[X|Y])\)
  • Covariance by Conditioning: \(\operatorname{Cov}(N,X)=\operatorname{Cov}(N,E[X|N])\)

Brownian Motion and Ito Calculus

  • Brownian Fixed-Time Distribution: \(W_t-W_s\sim N(0,t-s),\qquad W_t\sim N(0,t)\)
  • Ito Multiplication Table: \((dW_t)^2=dt,\qquad dt\,dW_t=0,\qquad (dt)^2=0\)
  • Quadratic Covariation with Correlation: \(dW_t^X dW_t^Y=\rho\,dt\)
  • Stochastic Integral Mean Zero: \(E\left[\int_0^tH_s\,dW_s\right]=0\)
  • Ito Isometry: \(E\left[\left(\int_0^tH_s\,dW_s\right)^2\right]=E\left[\int_0^tH_s^2\,ds\right]\)
  • Ito Formula, Time-Dependent: \(df(t,X_t)=f_tdt+f_xdX_t+\frac12f_{xx}(dX_t)^2\)
  • Ito Formula, One Variable: \(df(X_t)=f'(X_t)dX_t+\frac12f''(X_t)d\langle X\rangle_t\)
  • Ito Product Rule: \(d(X_tY_t)=X_tdY_t+Y_tdX_t+d\langle X,Y\rangle_t\)
  • Logarithm Rule for GBM: \(\frac{dS_t}{S_t}=\mu dt+\sigma dW_t\quad\Longrightarrow\quad d\log S_t=(\mu-\tfrac12\sigma^2)dt+\sigma dW_t\)
  • Brownian Exponential Martingale: \(M_t=\exp(\theta W_t-\tfrac12\theta^2t)\)
  • Diffusion Generator: \(dX_t=b(X_t)dt+\sqrt{a(X_t)}dW_t\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \mathcal Lf=b f'+\tfrac12a f'' \)
  • Scale Function Equation: \(b(x)s'(x)+\tfrac12a(x)s''(x)=0\)
  • Scale Function Integral Formula: \(s(x)=\int^x\exp\left(\int^y-\frac{2b(z)}{a(z)}\,dz\right)dy\)

Girsanov and Risk-Neutral Pricing

  • Stochastic Exponential Density: \(Z_t=\mathcal E\left(\int_0^t\theta_s\,dW_s\right),\qquad dZ_t=Z_t\theta_t\,dW_t\)
  • Girsanov Theorem: \(\frac{dQ}{dP}\Big|_{\mathcal F_t}=\mathcal E\left(\int_0^t\theta_s\,dW_s\right),\qquad \widetilde W_t=W_t-\int_0^t\theta_s\,ds\)
  • Constant-Theta Girsanov Form: \(\frac{dQ}{dP}=\exp(-\theta W_T-\tfrac12\theta^2T),\qquad W_t^Q=W_t+\theta t\)
  • Change of Measure Expectation: \(E^Q[Y]=E^P[Z_TY],\qquad Z_T=\frac{dQ}{dP}\Big|_{\mathcal F_T}\)
  • Risk-Neutral Drift Condition: \(\frac{dS_t}{S_t}=r\,dt+\text{volatility terms under }Q\)
  • Risk-Neutral Pricing: \(V_0=e^{-rT}E^Q[\text{payoff at }T]\)
  • Multidimensional Market Price of Risk: \(d\widetilde B_t^i=dB_t^i+\theta_i dt,\qquad dB_t^i=d\widetilde B_t^i-\theta_i dt\)
  • Correlated Brownian Decomposition: \(W_t^2=\rho B_t^1+\sqrt{1-\rho^2}\,B_t^2\)

Geometric Brownian Motion and Option Formulas

  • GBM Solution: \(\frac{dS_t}{S_t}=\mu dt+\sigma dW_t\quad\Longrightarrow\quad S_T=S_0\exp\left((\mu-\tfrac12\sigma^2)T+\sigma W_T\right)\)
  • Geometric Brownian Mean: \(\frac{dG_t}{G_t}=\alpha dt+\text{Brownian terms}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E[G_T]=G_0e^{\alpha T}\)
  • GBM Log Law: \(\frac{dR_t}{R_t}=m dt+\nu dB_t\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \log R_T\sim N\left(\log R_0+(m-\tfrac12\nu^2)T,\nu^2T\right)\)
  • Ratio of Correlated GBMs: \(R_t=S_t^1/S_t^2,\qquad \nu_R^2=\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2-2\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2,\qquad m_R=\sigma_2^2-\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2\)
  • Lognormal Truncated Expectation: \(E[R_T\mathbf 1_{\{R_T>K\}}]=R_0e^{mT}N(d_1),\qquad P(R_T>K)=N(d_2)\)
  • Call Payoff Formula: \(E[(R_T-K)^+]=R_0e^{mT}N(d_1)-KN(d_2)\)
  • Maximum Payoff Decomposition: \(\max(R_T,K)=R_T\mathbf 1_{\{R_T>K\}}+K\mathbf 1_{\{R_T\le K\}}\)

SDE Solution Tools and Special Densities

  • Linear ODE for Expectations: \(m'(t)=A+km(t),\quad m(0)=m_0\quad\Longrightarrow\quad m(t)=m_0e^{kt}+\frac{A}{k}(e^{kt}-1)\ (k\ne0)\)
  • Linear SDE Integrating Factor Pattern: \(dY_t=(c+kY_t)dt+\sigma Y_t dW_t\quad\Longrightarrow\quad Y_t=\Phi_t\left(Y_0+c\int_0^t\Phi_s^{-1}\,ds\right)\)

  • OU Stochastic Integral Variance: \(\operatorname{Var}\left(\delta e^{-\lambda T}\int_0^T e^{\lambda s}dW_s\right)=\frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda}(1-e^{-2\lambda T})\)
  • Square Transformation: \(Y_t=X_t^2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad dY_t=2X_t dX_t+(dX_t)^2\)
  • Reciprocal Transformation: \(X_t=1/S_t\quad\Longrightarrow\quad dX_t=-S_t^{-2}dS_t+S_t^{-3}(dS_t)^2\)

  • Brownian Bridge Drift to Zero: \(dW_t=-\frac{W_t}{T-t}dt+d\widetilde W_t,\qquad t<T\)

4. Formula Selection Guide

If the question gives or asks for...Use this formula path
The question asks for the density of a transformed variable.Use CDF and Density Relationship, then One-to-One or Many-to-One Density Transformation.
The transformation uses two variables such as a sum and ratio.Use Two-Dimensional Jacobian Formula.
You see independent gamma variables with the same rate.Use Gamma Sum Rule and Beta-Gamma Relationship.
A discrete variable mixes a continuous density.Use Discrete-Continuous Joint Law and Marginal Mixture Density.
The question asks for the conditional distribution of a discrete variable after observing a continuous value.Use Bayes Formula for Mixed Variables, then identify a Poisson or shifted Poisson form.
You need a mean or variance from conditioning.Use Law of Total Expectation and Law of Total Variance.
A stochastic process is transformed by square, log, reciprocal, or exponential.Use Ito Formula and the Ito Multiplication Table.
You need a second moment of a stochastic integral.Use Ito Isometry.
A new measure is defined by a density process.Use Stochastic Exponential Density and Girsanov Theorem.
You are pricing a derivative.Find risk-neutral dynamics, then use Risk-Neutral Pricing.
The payoff depends on a stock ratio.Use Ratio of Correlated GBMs, GBM Log Law, and lognormal option formulas.
A process has a linear SDE or expectation ODE.Use Linear SDE Integrating Factor Pattern or Linear ODE for Expectations.
A Brownian bridge density appears.Use Brownian Bridge Density and the matching Brownian Bridge Drift.

5. Formula Comparison

Similar formulasDifference and how to avoid confusion
CDF method vs density transformationCDF first finds a probability and differentiates. Density transformation uses inverse derivatives directly. Use CDF when the region has cases.
One-to-one vs many-to-one transformationsOne-to-one has one inverse value. Many-to-one has several roots and requires summing all contributions.
Gamma vs beta distributionsGamma lives on positive values. Beta lives on the interval (0,1). Ratios of same-rate gammas often become beta.
Geometric vs Pascal vs PoissonGeometric here starts at 1. Pascal here starts at 0 and has a binomial coefficient. Poisson starts at 0 and has e^{-m}m^n/n!.
Expectation vs variance by conditioningFor means, average conditional means. For variance, add average conditional variance and variance of conditional mean.
Ito formula vs ordinary chain ruleIto formula has the extra second-order term because (dW)^2=dt. Ordinary calculus misses this.
Girsanov sign conventionsAlways write the Brownian relation first. If W-tilde = W - integral theta ds, then dW = dW-tilde + theta dt.
Physical measure P vs risk-neutral measure QUse P for real-world dynamics if asked. Use Q for pricing, where tradable assets drift at r.
Call payoff vs maximum payoff(R_T-K)^+ pays zero below K. max(R_T,K) pays K below K. Their formulas differ.
GBM mean vs GBM log driftThe mean grows with proportional drift alpha. The log has drift alpha - volatility squared over 2.
