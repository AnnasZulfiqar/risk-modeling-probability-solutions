Paper 98720 - 2022/2023 - Final Exam 3/7/2023

Question 1

Complete question. Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be independent uniform random variables on \([0,1]\).

  1. Find \(P(Y\le zX)\) for all \(z\ge0\). Distinguish \(z\in[0,1]\) and \(z>1\).
  2. Obtain the density \(f_Z(z)\) of \(Z=Y/X\).
  3. Hence derive the density of the proportion \(X/(X+Y)\).

Topic and purpose. This tests geometric probability in the unit square, differentiating a cumulative distribution function, and transforming a random variable.

Rules used. If \((X,Y)\) is uniform on the square \([0,1]^2\), probabilities are areas. If \(F_Z(z)=P(Z\le z)\), then \(f_Z(z)=F_Z'(z)\) where the derivative exists. If \(W=g(Z)\), then \(f_W(w)=f_Z(g^{-1}(w))\left|\frac{d}{dw}g^{-1}(w)\right|\).

(a) Find \(P(Y\le zX)\)

\[ P(Y\le zX)=\text{area of }\{(x,y):0\le x\le1,\ 0\le y\le1,\ y\le zx\}. \]

Because the joint density is \(1\) on the unit square, probability equals area.

Case 1: \(0\le z\le1\). The line \(y=zx\) stays below \(y=1\) for every \(0\le x\le1\).

\[ P(Y\le zX)=\int_0^1 zx\,dx =z\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^1 =\frac z2. \]

We integrate the height \(zx\) of the triangle under the line.

Case 2: \(z>1\). The line \(y=zx\) reaches \(y=1\) when \(zx=1\), so \(x=1/z\).

\[ P(Y\le zX)=\int_0^{1/z} zx\,dx+\int_{1/z}^1 1\,dx. \]

For \(x\le1/z\), the height is \(zx\). For \(x>1/z\), the whole vertical slice of height \(1\) is included.

\[ \int_0^{1/z} zx\,dx =z\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^{1/z} =z\cdot\frac{1}{2z^2} =\frac{1}{2z}. \]

This is the small triangle below the line before it hits the top of the square.

\[ \int_{1/z}^1 1\,dx=1-\frac1z. \]

This is the rectangle-like part where all \(y\)-values from \(0\) to \(1\) satisfy the inequality.

\[ P(Y\le zX)=\frac{1}{2z}+1-\frac1z =1-\frac{1}{2z},\qquad z>1. \]

We combine the two areas.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer: \(z/2\) for \(z\in[0,1]\) and \(1-1/(2z)\) for \(z>1\).

(b) Density of \(Z=Y/X\)

\[ F_Z(z)=P(Z\le z)=P(Y/X\le z). \]

Since \(X>0\) except on a probability-zero event, we can multiply by \(X\).

\[ F_Z(z)=P(Y\le zX). \]

This is exactly the probability from part (a).

\[ F_Z(z)= \begin{cases} 0, & z<0,\\ z/2, & 0\le z\le1,\\ 1-\frac{1}{2z}, & z>1. \end{cases} \]

Ratios \(Y/X\) are nonnegative, so the CDF is \(0\) for \(z<0\).

\[ f_Z(z)=F_Z'(z)=\frac12,\qquad 0We differentiate \(z/2\).

\[ f_Z(z)=\frac{d}{dz}\left(1-\frac{1}{2z}\right) =\frac{1}{2z^2},\qquad z>1. \]

The derivative of \(-1/(2z)\) is \(1/(2z^2)\).

PDF check. This matches the printed density.

(c) Density of \(W=X/(X+Y)\)

\[ W=\frac{X}{X+Y}=\frac{1}{1+Y/X}=\frac{1}{1+Z}. \]

Dividing numerator and denominator by \(X\) expresses the proportion in terms of \(Z=Y/X\).

\[ w=\frac1{1+z} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad z=\frac{1-w}{w}. \]

We solve for the inverse transformation.

\[ \left|\frac{dz}{dw}\right| =\left|-\frac1{w^2}\right| =\frac1{w^2}. \]

This derivative is the Jacobian factor for the density transformation.

\[ z\le1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{1-w}{w}\le1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad w\ge\frac12. \]

This tells us which branch of \(f_Z\) to use.

\[ f_W(w)=f_Z\!\left(\frac{1-w}{w}\right)\frac1{w^2}. \]

We apply the transformation formula.

\[ f_W(w)=\frac12\cdot\frac1{w^2} =\frac{1}{2w^2},\qquad \frac12\le w\le1. \]

For \(w\ge1/2\), the corresponding \(z\le1\), so \(f_Z(z)=1/2\).

\[ f_W(w)= \frac{1}{2\left(\frac{1-w}{w}\right)^2}\cdot\frac1{w^2} =\frac{1}{2(1-w)^2},\qquad 0For \(w<1/2\), the corresponding \(z>1\), so we use \(f_Z(z)=1/(2z^2)\).

Common mistakes. The split point is \(w=1/2\), not \(z=1/2\). Always translate the interval through the inverse transformation.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

Question 2

Complete question. Let \(W_t\) be Brownian motion under probability \(P\), and let \(X_t=e^{W_t-t/2}\).

  1. Show that \(X_t\) is a martingale under \(P\).
  2. Calculate \(E^P[X_t]\) and \(\operatorname{Var}^P(X_t)\).
  3. Define \(Q\) by \(\left.\frac{dQ}{dP}\right|_t=X_t\). Use Girsanov's theorem to calculate \(E^P[W_te^{W_t}]\) and \(\operatorname{Var}^P(W_te^{W_t})\).

Topic and purpose. This tests the exponential martingale of Brownian motion, lognormal moments, and measure change.

Rules used. If \(W_t-W_s\sim N(0,t-s)\), then \(E[e^{u(W_t-W_s)}]=e^{u^2(t-s)/2}\). Girsanov says that if \(dQ/dP=\exp(W_t-t/2)\), then under \(Q\), \(\widetilde W_t=W_t-t\) is Brownian motion.

(a) Show \(X_t\) is a martingale

\[ E^P[X_t\mid\mathcal F_s] =E^P[e^{W_t-t/2}\mid\mathcal F_s],\qquad 0\le s\le t. \]

To prove the martingale property, we condition on the information available at time \(s\).

\[ W_t=W_s+(W_t-W_s). \]

We split Brownian motion into the known value \(W_s\) and the future increment.

\[ E^P[e^{W_t-t/2}\mid\mathcal F_s] =e^{W_s-t/2}E^P[e^{W_t-W_s}]. \]

The factor \(e^{W_s-t/2}\) is known at time \(s\), and the increment is independent of \(\mathcal F_s\).

\[ E^P[e^{W_t-W_s}]=e^{(t-s)/2}. \]

This is the moment generating function of a normal random variable with variance \(t-s\), evaluated at \(u=1\).

\[ E^P[X_t\mid\mathcal F_s] =e^{W_s-t/2}e^{(t-s)/2} =e^{W_s-s/2} =X_s. \]

The exponents combine because \(-t/2+(t-s)/2=-s/2\). This proves \(X_t\) is a martingale.

PDF check. This matches the printed proof.

(b) Mean and variance of \(X_t\)

\[ E^P[X_t]=E^P[X_0]=1. \]

A martingale has constant expectation, and \(X_0=e^0=1\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}^P(X_t)=E^P[X_t^2]-(E^P[X_t])^2. \]

Variance means second moment minus square of the mean.

\[ X_t^2=e^{2W_t-t}. \]

Squaring \(e^{W_t-t/2}\) doubles the exponent.

\[ E^P[X_t^2]=e^{-t}E^P[e^{2W_t}] =e^{-t}e^{(2^2)t/2} =e^{-t}e^{2t} =e^t. \]

We use \(W_t\sim N(0,t)\), so \(E[e^{uW_t}]=e^{u^2t/2}\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}^P(X_t)=e^t-1. \]

The mean is \(1\), so its square is \(1\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(c) Compute \(E^P[W_te^{W_t}]\) and \(\operatorname{Var}^P(W_te^{W_t})\)

\[ e^{W_t}=e^{t/2}e^{W_t-t/2}=e^{t/2}X_t. \]

We rewrite \(e^{W_t}\) using the density \(X_t=dQ/dP\).

\[ E^P[W_te^{W_t}] =e^{t/2}E^P[W_tX_t] =e^{t/2}E^Q[W_t]. \]

By the definition of \(Q\), \(E^P[H X_t]=E^Q[H]\).

\[ W_t=\widetilde W_t+t,\qquad E^Q[\widetilde W_t]=0. \]

Under \(Q\), \(\widetilde W_t=W_t-t\) is Brownian, so it has mean \(0\).

\[ E^P[W_te^{W_t}]=e^{t/2}t. \]

That gives the first required value.

\[ \operatorname{Var}^P(W_te^{W_t}) =E^P[W_t^2e^{2W_t}]-\left(E^P[W_te^{W_t}]\right)^2. \]

We need the second moment of \(W_te^{W_t}\).

\[ e^{2W_t}=e^{2t}e^{2W_t-2t}. \]

The factor \(e^{2W_t-2t}\) is another exponential martingale density.

Define \(Q'\) by \(dQ'/dP=e^{2W_t-2t}\). Under \(Q'\), \(\widehat W_t=W_t-2t\) is Brownian, so \(W_t=\widehat W_t+2t\).

\[ E^P[W_t^2e^{2W_t}] =e^{2t}E^{Q'}[W_t^2] =e^{2t}E^{Q'}[(\widehat W_t+2t)^2]. \]
\[ E^{Q'}[(\widehat W_t+2t)^2] =E[(\widehat W_t)^2]+4tE[\widehat W_t]+4t^2 =t+0+4t^2. \]

A Brownian motion at time \(t\) has mean \(0\) and variance \(t\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}^P(W_te^{W_t}) =e^{2t}(t+4t^2)-\left(te^{t/2}\right)^2 =e^{2t}(t+4t^2)-e^t t^2. \]

We subtract the square of the mean found earlier.

Common mistakes. Do not forget the deterministic factors \(e^{t/2}\) and \(e^{2t}\) when converting to the new probability measures.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

Question 3

Complete question. In a complete market with constant safe rate \(r\), under the risk-neutral measure \(Q\), two stocks \(X_t,Y_t\) follow

\[ \frac{dX_t}{X_t}=r\,dt+\sigma^X dW_t^X,\qquad \frac{dY_t}{Y_t}=r\,dt+\sigma^Y dW_t^Y,\qquad X_0=x,\ Y_0=y, \]

where \(W^X,W^Y\) have constant correlation \(\rho\). Let \(Z_t=X_t/Y_t\).

  1. Find the risk-neutral SDE for \(Z_t\), in the form \(dZ_t=a(Z_t)dW_t^X+b(Z_t)dW_t^Y+c(Z_t)dt\).
  2. Obtain the risk-neutral SDE for \(Z_t^2\).
  3. Find the time-0 prices of contracts paying \(Z_T\) and \(Z_T^2\).

Topic and purpose. This tests Ito's formula for a ratio, quadratic covariation, and risk-neutral pricing.

Rules used. For correlated Brownian motions, \(dW_t^XdW_t^Y=\rho\,dt\). Risk-neutral price at time \(0\) is \(e^{-rT}E^Q[\text{payoff}]\).

(a) SDE for \(Z_t=X_t/Y_t\)

\[ Z_t=X_tY_t^{-1}. \]

Writing the ratio this way lets us use Ito's product and inverse rules.

\[ \frac{dZ_t}{Z_t} =\frac{dX_t}{X_t}-\frac{dY_t}{Y_t} -\frac{d[X,Y]_t}{X_tY_t} +\frac{d[Y,Y]_t}{Y_t^2}. \]

This is the division rule. The last two terms are the second-order Brownian corrections.

\[ \frac{dX_t}{X_t}=r\,dt+\sigma^X dW_t^X,\qquad \frac{dY_t}{Y_t}=r\,dt+\sigma^Y dW_t^Y. \]

We substitute the given stock dynamics.

\[ \frac{d[X,Y]_t}{X_tY_t}=\sigma^X\sigma^Y\rho\,dt,\qquad \frac{d[Y,Y]_t}{Y_t^2}=(\sigma^Y)^2dt. \]

The covariance term uses the correlation \(\rho\); the variance term uses \(dW^Y dW^Y=dt\).

\[ \frac{dZ_t}{Z_t} =\sigma^X dW_t^X-\sigma^Y dW_t^Y +\sigma^Y(\sigma^Y-\sigma^X\rho)dt. \]

The \(r\,dt\) terms cancel because both stocks have the same risk-neutral drift.

\[ dZ_t=\sigma^X Z_t\,dW_t^X-\sigma^Y Z_t\,dW_t^Y +\sigma^Y(\sigma^Y-\sigma^X\rho)Z_t\,dt. \]

Multiplying by \(Z_t\) gives the requested form.

\[ a(z)=\sigma^Xz,\qquad b(z)=-\sigma^Yz,\qquad c(z)=\sigma^Y(\sigma^Y-\sigma^X\rho)z. \]

These are the functions requested by the problem.

PDF check. This matches the printed SDE.

(b) SDE for \(Z_t^2\)

\[ d(Z_t^2)=2Z_t\,dZ_t+(dZ_t)^2. \]

This is Ito's formula for the square function.

\[ \frac{d(Z_t^2)}{Z_t^2} =2\frac{dZ_t}{Z_t}+\frac{d[Z,Z]_t}{Z_t^2}. \]

Dividing by \(Z_t^2\) makes the calculation cleaner.

\[ \frac{d[Z,Z]_t}{Z_t^2} = (\sigma^X)^2dt+(\sigma^Y)^2dt-2\sigma^X\sigma^Y\rho\,dt. \]

The minus sign appears because the \(dW^Y\) coefficient in \(dZ/Z\) is \(-\sigma^Y\).

\[ \frac{d(Z_t^2)}{Z_t^2} = 2\sigma^X dW_t^X-2\sigma^Y dW_t^Y +2\sigma^Y(\sigma^Y-\sigma^X\rho)dt +\left((\sigma^X)^2+(\sigma^Y)^2-2\sigma^X\sigma^Y\rho\right)dt. \]

We substitute the SDE from part (a) and add the quadratic variation term.

\[ \frac{d(Z_t^2)}{Z_t^2} = 2\sigma^X dW_t^X-2\sigma^Y dW_t^Y +\left((\sigma^X)^2+3(\sigma^Y)^2-4\sigma^X\sigma^Y\rho\right)dt. \]

We collect the drift terms.

PDF check. This matches the printed SDE.

(c) Prices of payoffs \(Z_T\) and \(Z_T^2\)

\[ Z_0=\frac{x}{y}. \]

The initial value of the ratio matters in the price.

\[ \mu_Z=\sigma^Y(\sigma^Y-\sigma^X\rho). \]

This is the drift coefficient in \(dZ_t/Z_t\).

\[ E^Q[Z_T]=Z_0e^{\mu_ZT}. \]

A geometric Brownian motion with proportional drift \(\mu_Z\) has mean equal to initial value times \(e^{\mu_ZT}\).

\[ \text{Price}(Z_T)=e^{-rT}E^Q[Z_T] =\frac{x}{y}e^{(-r+\sigma^Y(\sigma^Y-\sigma^X\rho))T}. \]

We discount the risk-neutral expectation.

\[ \mu_{Z^2}=(\sigma^X)^2+3(\sigma^Y)^2-4\sigma^X\sigma^Y\rho. \]

This is the drift coefficient in \(d(Z_t^2)/Z_t^2\).

\[ E^Q[Z_T^2]=Z_0^2e^{\mu_{Z^2}T}. \]

The initial value of \(Z_t^2\) is \(Z_0^2=(x/y)^2\).

\[ \text{Price}(Z_T^2)=e^{-rT}E^Q[Z_T^2] =\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)^2e^{(-r+(\sigma^X)^2+3(\sigma^Y)^2-4\sigma^X\sigma^Y\rho)T}. \]

Again, we discount under the risk-neutral measure.

PDF correction. The printed solution states the general formula \(e^{-rT}E[G_T]=G_0e^{(\alpha-r)T}\), which is correct. It then omits \(G_0=Z_0=x/y\) and \(G_0=Z_0^2=(x/y)^2\) in the final two prices. The corrected prices are the two formulas above. If \(x=y\), the printed final lines match because \(Z_0=1\).

Common mistakes. Do not forget initial values in geometric Brownian motion expectations. The drift determines growth from the starting value; it does not replace the starting value.

Question 1

Complete question. The beta distribution with parameters \(\alpha,\beta>0\) has density

\[ f(x)=x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)},\qquad 0\le x\le1. \]

If \(X\) has this distribution, find \(E[X]\), \(\operatorname{Var}(X)\), the density of \(Y=X/(1-X)\), and the density of \(Z=\log Y\).

Topic and purpose. This tests beta moments, gamma-function identities, and transformations to the beta-prime and logit-beta distributions.

Rules used. The beta integral is \(\int_0^1x^{u-1}(1-x)^{v-1}\,dx=\Gamma(u)\Gamma(v)/\Gamma(u+v)\). Also \(\Gamma(q+1)=q\Gamma(q)\). For transformations, multiply by the absolute derivative of the inverse.

(a) Expectation of \(X\)

\[ E[X]=\int_0^1 x f(x)\,dx. \]

Expectation is the integral of value times density.

\[ E[X]=\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \int_0^1x^\alpha(1-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx. \]

Multiplying by \(x\) changes \(x^{\alpha-1}\) into \(x^\alpha\).

\[ \int_0^1x^\alpha(1-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx =\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)}. \]

This is the beta integral with \(u=\alpha+1\) and \(v=\beta\).

\[ E[X]= \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)}. \]

We substitute the beta integral.

\[ \Gamma(\alpha+1)=\alpha\Gamma(\alpha),\qquad \Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)=(\alpha+\beta)\Gamma(\alpha+\beta). \]

We use the gamma recursion formula.

\[ E[X]=\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}. \]

All matching gamma factors cancel.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(b) Variance of \(X\)

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X)=E[X^2]-(E[X])^2. \]

We first need the second moment.

\[ E[X^2]= \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \int_0^1x^{\alpha+1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx. \]

Multiplying by \(x^2\) changes \(x^{\alpha-1}\) into \(x^{\alpha+1}\).

\[ \int_0^1x^{\alpha+1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx =\frac{\Gamma(\alpha+2)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+2)}. \]

This is the beta integral with \(u=\alpha+2\).

\[ \Gamma(\alpha+2)=(\alpha+1)\alpha\Gamma(\alpha), \] \[ \Gamma(\alpha+\beta+2)=(\alpha+\beta+1)(\alpha+\beta)\Gamma(\alpha+\beta). \]

We expand the gamma functions by two steps.

\[ E[X^2]=\frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{(\alpha+\beta)(\alpha+\beta+1)}. \]

After cancellation, this is the second moment.

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X)= \frac{\alpha(\alpha+1)}{(\alpha+\beta)(\alpha+\beta+1)} -\left(\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}\right)^2. \]

We subtract the square of the mean.

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X)= \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)}. \]

Putting the fractions over a common denominator gives the standard beta variance formula.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(c) Density of \(Y=X/(1-X)\)

\[ y=\frac{x}{1-x} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x=\frac{y}{1+y}. \]

We solve the transformation for \(x\).

\[ \frac{dx}{dy}=\frac{1}{(1+y)^2}. \]

This derivative is the Jacobian factor.

\[ 1-x=1-\frac{y}{1+y}=\frac{1}{1+y}. \]

We will substitute this into the beta density.

\[ f_Y(y)=f_X\!\left(\frac{y}{1+y}\right)\frac{1}{(1+y)^2},\qquad y>0. \]

This is the change-of-variables formula.

\[ f_Y(y)= \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \left(\frac{y}{1+y}\right)^{\alpha-1} \left(\frac{1}{1+y}\right)^{\beta-1} \frac{1}{(1+y)^2}. \]

We substitute \(x=y/(1+y)\) and \(1-x=1/(1+y)\).

\[ f_Y(y)= \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{y^{\alpha-1}}{(1+y)^{\alpha+\beta}}, \qquad y>0. \]

All powers of \(1+y\) combine to \(\alpha+\beta\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(d) Density of \(Z=\log Y\)

\[ z=\log y \quad\Longrightarrow\quad y=e^z,\qquad -\inftyTaking a logarithm maps \(y>0\) to all real \(z\).

\[ \frac{dy}{dz}=e^z. \]

This is the Jacobian factor.

\[ f_Z(z)=f_Y(e^z)e^z. \]

We substitute \(y=e^z\) into the density of \(Y\).

\[ f_Z(z)= \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{(e^z)^{\alpha-1}}{(1+e^z)^{\alpha+\beta}}e^z. \]

We plug in the formula from part (c).

\[ f_Z(z)= \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)} \frac{e^{\alpha z}}{(1+e^z)^{\alpha+\beta}}, \qquad z\in\mathbb R. \]

The powers combine because \(e^{(\alpha-1)z}e^z=e^{\alpha z}\).

Common mistakes. Do not forget the extra \(e^z\) from \(dy/dz\). Without it the density will not integrate to \(1\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

Question 2

Complete question. Let \(X_t\) be an Ito process

\[ dX_t=b(X_t)dt+\sqrt{a(X_t)}\,dW_t,\qquad X_0=x, \]

where \(a\) is strictly positive. Show:

  1. If \(M_t=s(X_t)\) is a local martingale for \(s\in C^2\), then \(b(x)s'(x)+a(x)s''(x)/2=0\).
  2. \(s(x)=\int^x e^{\int^y -2b(z)/a(z)\,dz}\,dy\) solves the equation, so \(s(X_t)\) is a local martingale.
  3. \(M_t=e^{-(2\mu/\sigma^2)X_t}\) is a martingale if \(X_t=\mu t+\sigma W_t\).
  4. \(M_t=X_t^{1-2\mu/\sigma^2}\) is a martingale if \(X_t=e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t+\sigma W_t}\).

Topic and purpose. This tests the generator of a diffusion, scale functions, and applying Ito's formula to build martingales.

Rules used. If \(dX_t=b(X_t)dt+\sqrt{a(X_t)}dW_t\), the infinitesimal generator is \(\mathcal L f(x)=b(x)f'(x)+\frac12a(x)f''(x)\). A process \(f(X_t)\) has zero drift exactly when \(\mathcal L f=0\).

(a) Derive the differential equation

\[ M_t=s(X_t). \]

The function \(s\) is twice differentiable, so Ito's formula applies.

\[ dM_t=s'(X_t)dX_t+\frac12s''(X_t)d\langle X\rangle_t. \]

Ito's formula has a first derivative term and a second derivative term.

\[ d\langle X\rangle_t=a(X_t)dt. \]

The diffusion coefficient is \(\sqrt{a(X_t)}\), and its square is \(a(X_t)\).

\[ dM_t= \left[b(X_t)s'(X_t)+\frac12a(X_t)s''(X_t)\right]dt +s'(X_t)\sqrt{a(X_t)}\,dW_t. \]

We substitute the SDE for \(dX_t\).

\[ b(x)s'(x)+\frac{a(x)}2s''(x)=0. \]

A local martingale cannot have a nonzero drift term, so the bracketed drift must vanish.

PDF check. This matches the printed equation.

(b) Verify the scale function

\[ s(x)=\int^x \exp\left(\int^y-\frac{2b(z)}{a(z)}\,dz\right)dy. \]

The lower limits are arbitrary constants. Changing them only adds or multiplies by constants, which does not affect the zero-drift property.

\[ s'(x)=\exp\left(\int^x-\frac{2b(z)}{a(z)}\,dz\right). \]

By the fundamental theorem of calculus, the derivative of an integral up to \(x\) is the integrand at \(x\).

\[ s''(x)= \left(-\frac{2b(x)}{a(x)}\right) \exp\left(\int^x-\frac{2b(z)}{a(z)}\,dz\right). \]

We differentiate the exponential using the chain rule.

\[ b(x)s'(x)+\frac{a(x)}2s''(x) = b(x)e^{\int^x-2b/a} +\frac{a(x)}2\left(-\frac{2b(x)}{a(x)}\right)e^{\int^x-2b/a}. \]

This line substitutes \(s'\) and \(s''\). The shorthand \(e^{\int^x-2b/a}\) means the same exponential as above.

\[ b(x)e^{\int^x-2b/a}-b(x)e^{\int^x-2b/a}=0. \]

The two terms cancel exactly, so \(s\) solves the differential equation.

PDF check. This matches the printed verification.

(c) Martingale for arithmetic Brownian motion

\[ X_t=\mu t+\sigma W_t \quad\Longrightarrow\quad dX_t=\mu\,dt+\sigma\,dW_t. \]

This process has constant drift \(\mu\) and constant volatility \(\sigma\).

\[ M_t=e^{-\frac{2\mu}{\sigma^2}X_t}. \]

We apply Ito's formula to this exponential function.

\[ f'(x)=-\frac{2\mu}{\sigma^2}f(x),\qquad f''(x)=\frac{4\mu^2}{\sigma^4}f(x). \]

These are the first and second derivatives of \(f(x)=e^{-2\mu x/\sigma^2}\).

\[ dM_t= \left[-\frac{2\mu}{\sigma^2}\mu+\frac12\frac{4\mu^2}{\sigma^4}\sigma^2\right]M_tdt -\frac{2\mu}{\sigma^2}\sigma M_t\,dW_t. \]

We substitute \(dX_t\) and \((dX_t)^2=\sigma^2dt\).

\[ -\frac{2\mu^2}{\sigma^2}+\frac{2\mu^2}{\sigma^2}=0. \]

The drift cancels.

\[ dM_t=-\frac{2\mu}{\sigma}M_t\,dW_t. \]

Only a stochastic integral remains, so \(M_t\) is a local martingale. Its lognormal moments are finite on finite time intervals, so it is a martingale.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(d) Martingale for geometric Brownian motion

\[ X_t=e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t+\sigma W_t}. \]

This is the explicit solution of a geometric Brownian motion.

\[ dX_t=\mu X_tdt+\sigma X_tdW_t. \]

Ito's formula gives this SDE from the exponential expression.

\[ \gamma=1-\frac{2\mu}{\sigma^2},\qquad M_t=X_t^\gamma. \]

We name the exponent \(\gamma\) to simplify the algebra.

\[ dM_t=\gamma X_t^{\gamma-1}dX_t+\frac12\gamma(\gamma-1)X_t^{\gamma-2}(dX_t)^2. \]

This is Ito's formula for \(x^\gamma\).

\[ (dX_t)^2=\sigma^2X_t^2dt. \]

The Brownian part \(\sigma X_tdW_t\) is squared.

\[ dM_t= \gamma X_t^\gamma(\mu dt+\sigma dW_t) +\frac12\gamma(\gamma-1)\sigma^2X_t^\gamma dt. \]

We substitute \(dX_t\) and simplify powers of \(X_t\).

\[ \text{drift coefficient} = \gamma\mu+\frac12\gamma(\gamma-1)\sigma^2 = \gamma\left(\mu+\frac12\left(-\frac{2\mu}{\sigma^2}\right)\sigma^2\right)=0. \]

Since \(\gamma-1=-2\mu/\sigma^2\), the drift cancels.

\[ dM_t=\gamma\sigma X_t^\gamma\,dW_t. \]

Only the Brownian term remains, so \(M_t\) is a martingale under the usual finite-moment condition.

Common mistakes. In part (d), do not forget that \(dX_t\) already contains \(X_t\). Missing this causes the powers of \(X_t\) to be wrong.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

Question 3

Complete question. The stock price \(S_t\) and volatility \(\sigma_t\) follow

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t}=(\nu\sigma_t+r)dt+\sigma_t\,dW_t,\qquad d\sigma_t=-\lambda\sigma_tdt+\delta\,dW_t, \]

with constants \(\delta,\lambda,\nu,r\). Find the risk-neutral dynamics of \(S_t\), the risk-neutral dynamics of \(\sigma_t\), and the prices of contracts paying \(\sigma_T\) and \(\sigma_T^2\).

Topic and purpose. This tests risk-neutral drift adjustment with one Brownian driver, solving an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck-type volatility SDE, and pricing by discounted expectation.

(a) Risk-neutral stock dynamics

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t}=(\nu\sigma_t+r)dt+\sigma_t\,dW_t. \]

Under \(P\), the stock has excess drift \(\nu\sigma_t\).

\[ d\widetilde W_t=dW_t+\nu\,dt \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad dW_t=d\widetilde W_t-\nu\,dt. \]

This is the Girsanov change that removes the excess stock drift.

\[ \frac{dS_t}{S_t} =(\nu\sigma_t+r)dt+\sigma_t(d\widetilde W_t-\nu dt) =r\,dt+\sigma_t\,d\widetilde W_t. \]

The \(\nu\sigma_tdt\) terms cancel.

PDF check. This matches the printed risk-neutral stock dynamics.

(b) Risk-neutral volatility dynamics

\[ d\sigma_t=-\lambda\sigma_tdt+\delta\,dW_t. \]

We now use the same Brownian substitution because the same \(W_t\) drives both equations.

\[ d\sigma_t=-\lambda\sigma_tdt+\delta(d\widetilde W_t-\nu dt). \]

Replace \(dW_t\) by \(d\widetilde W_t-\nu dt\).

\[ d\sigma_t=(-\lambda\sigma_t-\delta\nu)dt+\delta\,d\widetilde W_t. \]

The volatility drift is shifted by \(-\delta\nu\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(c) Price of payoff \(\sigma_T\)

\[ d\sigma_t=(-\lambda\sigma_t-\delta\nu)dt+\delta\,d\widetilde W_t. \]

This is a linear SDE.

\[ d(e^{\lambda t}\sigma_t) =e^{\lambda t}d\sigma_t+\lambda e^{\lambda t}\sigma_tdt. \]

We multiply by the integrating factor \(e^{\lambda t}\).

\[ d(e^{\lambda t}\sigma_t) =-\delta\nu e^{\lambda t}dt+\delta e^{\lambda t}d\widetilde W_t. \]

The \(-\lambda\sigma_t\) and \(+\lambda\sigma_t\) terms cancel.

\[ e^{\lambda T}\sigma_T =\sigma_0-\delta\nu\int_0^T e^{\lambda s}ds +\delta\int_0^T e^{\lambda s}d\widetilde W_s. \]

We integrate from \(0\) to \(T\).

\[ \int_0^T e^{\lambda s}ds=\frac{e^{\lambda T}-1}{\lambda}. \]

This is the ordinary exponential integral, assuming \(\lambda\ne0\).

\[ \sigma_T =e^{-\lambda T}\sigma_0-\frac{\delta\nu}{\lambda}(1-e^{-\lambda T}) +\delta e^{-\lambda T}\int_0^T e^{\lambda s}d\widetilde W_s. \]

We multiply by \(e^{-\lambda T}\).

\[ E^Q[\sigma_T] =e^{-\lambda T}\sigma_0-\frac{\delta\nu}{\lambda}(1-e^{-\lambda T}). \]

The stochastic integral has mean zero.

\[ \text{Price}(\sigma_T) =e^{-rT}\left(e^{-\lambda T}\sigma_0-\frac{\delta\nu}{\lambda}(1-e^{-\lambda T})\right). \]

Risk-neutral pricing discounts the expected payoff.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(d) Price of payoff \(\sigma_T^2\)

\[ A=e^{-\lambda T}\sigma_0-\frac{\delta\nu}{\lambda}(1-e^{-\lambda T}), \] \[ B=\delta e^{-\lambda T}\int_0^T e^{\lambda s}d\widetilde W_s. \]

From part (c), \(\sigma_T=A+B\), where \(A\) is deterministic and \(B\) has mean zero.

\[ E^Q[\sigma_T^2]=E[(A+B)^2]=A^2+2AE[B]+E[B^2]=A^2+\operatorname{Var}(B). \]

The middle term vanishes because \(E[B]=0\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}(B) =\delta^2e^{-2\lambda T}\int_0^T e^{2\lambda s}ds. \]

We use Ito isometry for the stochastic integral.

\[ \int_0^T e^{2\lambda s}ds=\frac{e^{2\lambda T}-1}{2\lambda}. \]

This is another ordinary exponential integral.

\[ \operatorname{Var}(B)=\frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda}(1-e^{-2\lambda T}). \]

Multiplying by \(e^{-2\lambda T}\) changes \(e^{2\lambda T}-1\) into \(1-e^{-2\lambda T}\).

\[ \text{Price}(\sigma_T^2) =e^{-rT}\left[ \left(e^{-\lambda T}\sigma_0-\frac{\delta\nu}{\lambda}(1-e^{-\lambda T})\right)^2 +\frac{\delta^2}{2\lambda}(1-e^{-2\lambda T}) \right]. \]

We discount the second moment under the risk-neutral measure.

Common mistakes. Do not square only the mean. The random stochastic-integral term contributes its variance to \(E[\sigma_T^2]\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

Question 1

Complete question. Let \(r\in\{1,2,\ldots\}\), \(00\). Suppose \(N\) has the Pascal distribution

\[ P(N=n)=\binom{n+r-1}{n}(1-p)^rp^n,\qquad n=0,1,2,\ldots. \]

Conditionally on \(N=n\), \(X\) has density

\[ f_{X|N=n}(x)=\frac{\beta^{r+n}}{\Gamma(r+n)}x^{r+n-1}e^{-\beta x},\qquad x>0. \]
  1. Find the unconditional density of \(X\) and identify its distribution.
  2. Find the conditional distribution of \(N\) given \(X=x\).
  3. Compute \(E[N|X]\) and \(E[X|N]\).
  4. Deduce \(E[N]\), \(E[X]\), and \(\operatorname{Cov}(N,X)\).

Topic and purpose. This tests a negative-binomial/gamma mixture, Bayes' formula, conditional expectation, and covariance by conditioning.

(a) Unconditional density of \(X\)

\[ f_X(x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x). \]

Because \(N\) is discrete, we sum the joint density over all possible \(n\).

\[ P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x) = \binom{n+r-1}{n}(1-p)^rp^n \frac{\beta^{r+n}}{\Gamma(r+n)}x^{r+n-1}e^{-\beta x}. \]

We multiply the Pascal probability by the conditional gamma density.

\[ \binom{n+r-1}{n}=\frac{\Gamma(r+n)}{\Gamma(r)n!}. \]

This gamma-function identity rewrites the binomial coefficient.

\[ P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x) = \frac{(1-p)^r\beta^r}{\Gamma(r)} x^{r-1}e^{-\beta x} \frac{(p\beta x)^n}{n!}. \]

The \(\Gamma(r+n)\) factors cancel, and the \(n\)-dependent part becomes a power series term.

\[ f_X(x)= \frac{(1-p)^r\beta^r}{\Gamma(r)} x^{r-1}e^{-\beta x} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(p\beta x)^n}{n!}. \]

We factor out everything that does not depend on \(n\).

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(p\beta x)^n}{n!}=e^{p\beta x}. \]

This is the exponential series.

\[ f_X(x)= \frac{(\beta(1-p))^r}{\Gamma(r)} x^{r-1}e^{-\beta(1-p)x},\qquad x>0. \]

The exponential simplifies because \(e^{-\beta x}e^{p\beta x}=e^{-\beta(1-p)x}\).

\[ X\sim\Gamma(r,\beta(1-p)). \]

This is a gamma density with shape \(r\) and rate \(\beta(1-p)\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(b) Conditional distribution of \(N\) given \(X=x\)

\[ P(N=n|X=x)=\frac{P(N=n)f_{X|N=n}(x)}{f_X(x)}. \]

This is Bayes' formula for a discrete-continuous pair.

\[ P(N=n|X=x) = \frac{ \frac{(1-p)^r\beta^r}{\Gamma(r)}x^{r-1}e^{-\beta x}\frac{(p\beta x)^n}{n!} }{ \frac{(1-p)^r\beta^r}{\Gamma(r)}x^{r-1}e^{-\beta(1-p)x} }. \]

We substitute the numerator from part (a) and the marginal density.

\[ P(N=n|X=x)=e^{-p\beta x}\frac{(p\beta x)^n}{n!}. \]

The constants cancel, and the exponential becomes \(e^{-\beta x+\beta(1-p)x}=e^{-p\beta x}\).

\[ N|(X=x)\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(p\beta x). \]

This is exactly the Poisson mass function with mean \(p\beta x\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(c) Conditional expectations

\[ N|(X=x)\sim\operatorname{Poisson}(p\beta x). \]

A Poisson random variable has mean equal to its parameter.

\[ E[N|X=x]=p\beta x, \qquad E[N|X]=p\beta X. \]

We replace the fixed \(x\) by the random variable \(X\).

\[ X|(N=n)\sim\Gamma(r+n,\beta). \]

This is given in the question.

\[ E[X|N=n]=\frac{r+n}{\beta}. \]

A gamma variable with shape \(k\) and rate \(\beta\) has mean \(k/\beta\).

\[ E[X|N]=\frac{r+N}{\beta}. \]

We replace \(n\) by the random variable \(N\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(d) \(E[N]\), \(E[X]\), and \(\operatorname{Cov}(N,X)\)

\[ X\sim\Gamma(r,\beta(1-p)). \]

We use the marginal distribution found in part (a).

\[ E[X]=\frac{r}{\beta(1-p)}. \]

A gamma variable with shape \(r\) and rate \(\beta(1-p)\) has mean \(r/[\beta(1-p)]\).

\[ E[N]=E[E[N|X]]=E[p\beta X]=p\beta E[X]. \]

This is the law of total expectation.

\[ E[N]=p\beta\cdot\frac{r}{\beta(1-p)} =\frac{rp}{1-p}. \]

The \(\beta\) factors cancel.

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(N,X)=\operatorname{Cov}(N,E[X|N]). \]

This identity holds because the remaining conditional noise in \(X\) has zero covariance with any function of \(N\).

\[ E[X|N]=\frac{r+N}{\beta}. \]

We substitute the conditional expectation from part (c).

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(N,X)=\operatorname{Cov}\left(N,\frac{r+N}{\beta}\right) =\frac1\beta\operatorname{Cov}(N,N) =\frac{\operatorname{Var}(N)}{\beta}. \]

The constant \(r\) has zero covariance with \(N\).

\[ \operatorname{Var}(N)=\frac{rp}{(1-p)^2}. \]

This is the variance of the Pascal distribution in this parametrization.

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(N,X)=\frac{rp}{\beta(1-p)^2}. \]

We divide the variance of \(N\) by \(\beta\).

Common mistakes. Do not confuse this Pascal distribution, supported on \(0,1,2,\ldots\), with the geometric distribution supported on \(1,2,\ldots\). The means differ by a shift.

Question 2

Complete question. Let \(T>0\), \(a>0\), and \(W_t\) be Brownian motion. Define

\[ Z_t=\frac{a+T-t+W_t^2}{a+T},\qquad 0\le t\le T. \]
  1. Show that \(Z_t\) is a strictly positive martingale with \(Z_0=1\).
  2. Let \(Q\) be defined by \(dQ/dP=Z_T\). Find a Brownian motion under \(Q\).
  3. Find the dynamics of \(W\) under \(Q\).
  4. Compute \(E^Q[W_T^2]\).

Topic and purpose. This tests using Ito's formula to build a martingale density and then applying Girsanov's theorem.

(a) Strict positivity and martingale property

\[ a+T-t+W_t^2\ge a>0,\qquad 0\le t\le T. \]

Since \(a>0\), the numerator is always positive.

\[ Z_t>0,\qquad Z_0=\frac{a+T+0}{a+T}=1. \]

This proves strict positivity and the initial value.

\[ d(W_t^2)=2W_t\,dW_t+dt. \]

This is Ito's formula for the square of Brownian motion.

\[ d(a+T-t+W_t^2)=-dt+(2W_t\,dW_t+dt)=2W_t\,dW_t. \]

The \(-dt\) and \(+dt\) cancel.

\[ dZ_t=\frac{2W_t}{a+T}\,dW_t. \]

The denominator \(a+T\) is constant.

\[ E\left[\int_0^T\left(\frac{2W_t}{a+T}\right)^2dt\right] =\frac{4}{(a+T)^2}\int_0^T E[W_t^2]dt =\frac{4}{(a+T)^2}\int_0^T t\,dt<\infty. \]

This square-integrability check means the stochastic integral is a true martingale.

\[ Z_t\text{ is a strictly positive martingale.} \]

It starts at \(1\) and has only a Brownian integral term with enough integrability.

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(b) Brownian motion under \(Q\)

\[ dZ_t=\frac{2W_t}{a+T}\,dW_t. \]

To use Girsanov, we rewrite this as \(dZ_t=Z_t\theta_t\,dW_t\).

\[ Z_t=\frac{a+T-t+W_t^2}{a+T}. \]

We divide the coefficient of \(dW_t\) by \(Z_t\).

\[ \theta_t=\frac{2W_t/(a+T)}{(a+T-t+W_t^2)/(a+T)} =\frac{2W_t}{a+T-t+W_t^2}. \]

The denominator \(a+T\) cancels.

\[ \widetilde W_t=W_t-\int_0^t\frac{2W_s}{a+T-s+W_s^2}\,ds. \]

By Girsanov's theorem, this process is Brownian motion under \(Q\).

PDF check. This matches the printed answer.

(c) Dynamics of \(W\) under \(Q\)

\[ d\widetilde W_t=dW_t-\frac{2W_t}{a+T-t+W_t^2}dt. \]

This is the differential version of part (b).

\[ dW_t=\frac{2W_t}{a+T-t+W_t^2}dt+d\widetilde W_t. \]

We solve for \(dW_t\).

\[ W_0=0. \]

Brownian motion starts from zero.

PDF check. This matches the printed dynamics.

(d) Compute \(E^Q[W_T^2]\)

\[ E^Q[W_T^2]=E^P[Z_TW_T^2]. \]

This is the definition of the measure \(Q\): expectation under \(Q\) equals expectation under \(P\) weighted by \(Z_T\).

\[ Z_T=\frac{a+W_T^2}{a+T}. \]

At time \(T\), the term \(T-T\) is zero.

\[ E^Q[W_T^2]=\frac{1}{a+T}E^P[(a+W_T^2)W_T^2]. \]

We substitute \(Z_T\).

\[ E^P[(a+W_T^2)W_T^2]=aE[W_T^2]+E[W_T^4]. \]

We expand the product.

\[ W_T\sim N(0,T),\qquad E[W_T^2]=T,\qquad E[W_T^4]=3T^2. \]

These are the second and fourth moments of a centered normal variable with variance \(T\).

\[ E^Q[W_T^2]=\frac{aT+3T^2}{a+T}. \]

We substitute the normal moments.

Common mistakes. Do not use \(E^Q[W_T^2]=T\). Under \(Q\), \(W\) is no longer a standard Brownian motion; \(\widetilde W\) is.

Question 3

Complete question. Let \(a\in\mathbb R\), \(c>0\), \(\sigma>0\), \(x>0\), and let \(X_t>0\) satisfy

\[ dX_t=\left(aX_t+\frac{c}{X_t}\right)dt+\sigma X_t\,dW_t,\qquad X_0=x. \]
  1. Let \(Y_t=X_t^2\). Show that \(Y_t\) satisfies a linear SDE.

(a) SDE for \(Y_t=X_t^2\)

\[ Y_t=X_t^2. \]

We square \(X_t\) because the term \(c/X_t\) becomes simpler after multiplying by \(X_t\).

\[ dY_t=2X_t\,dX_t+(dX_t)^2. \]

\[ 2X_t\,dX_t =2X_t\left(aX_t+\frac{c}{X_t}\right)dt+2\sigma X_t^2dW_t =(2aX_t^2+2c)dt+2\sigma X_t^2dW_t. \]

Since \(Y_t=X_t^2\), the equation is linear in \(Y_t\).

Common Exam Mistakes Across These Papers

  • For ratio distributions, forgetting that different regions require different formulas.
  • Dropping the Jacobian factor in a density transformation.
  • Using the physical drift instead of the risk-neutral drift when pricing.
  • Forgetting initial values such as \(Z_0=x/y\) in geometric Brownian motion expectations.
  • Missing the Ito correction term \(\frac12 f''(X_t)(dX_t)^2\).
  • Thinking \(W\) is still Brownian after a measure change; the shifted process is Brownian.
  • Squaring a random payoff by squaring its mean and forgetting its variance.

Formulae Used

  • Uniform area rule: for uniform \((X,Y)\) on \([0,1]^2\), probability equals area.
  • CDF to density: \(f_Z(z)=F_Z'(z)\).
  • Change of variables: \(f_Y(y)=f_X(g^{-1}(y))\left|\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|\).
  • Normal exponential moment: if \(W_t\sim N(0,t)\), \(E[e^{uW_t}]=e^{u^2t/2}\).
  • Brownian exponential martingale: \(e^{\theta W_t-\theta^2t/2}\).
  • Girsanov: if \(dQ/dP=\mathcal E(\int\theta_t\,dW_t)\), then \(\widetilde W_t=W_t-\int_0^t\theta_sds\) is Brownian under \(Q\).
  • Ito rules: \((dW_t)^2=dt\), \(dt\,dW_t=0\), and \((dt)^2=0\).
  • Ito formula: \(df(X_t)=f'(X_t)dX_t+\frac12f''(X_t)d\langle X\rangle_t\).
  • Quadratic covariation with correlation: \(dW_t^XdW_t^Y=\rho\,dt\).
  • Risk-neutral pricing: \(\text{price}=e^{-rT}E^Q[\text{payoff}]\).
  • Geometric Brownian mean: if \(dG_t/G_t=\alpha dt+\text{Brownian terms}\), then \(E[G_T]=G_0e^{\alpha T}\).
  • Beta integral: \(\int_0^1x^{u-1}(1-x)^{v-1}dx=\Gamma(u)\Gamma(v)/\Gamma(u+v)\).
  • Gamma recursion: \(\Gamma(q+1)=q\Gamma(q)\).
  • Beta mean: \(E[X]=\alpha/(\alpha+\beta)\).
  • Beta variance: \(\operatorname{Var}(X)=\alpha\beta/[(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)]\).
  • Generator of \(dX_t=b(X_t)dt+\sqrt{a(X_t)}dW_t\): \(\mathcal Lf=b f'+\frac12a f''\).
  • Gamma density with shape \(k\), rate \(\lambda\): \(f(x)=\lambda^kx^{k-1}e^{-\lambda x}/\Gamma(k)\).
  • Gamma mean: \(k/\lambda\). Gamma variance: \(k/\lambda^2\).
  • Poisson mass: \(P(N=n)=e^{-m}m^n/n!\). Poisson mean: \(m\).
  • Pascal mean and variance here: \(E[N]=rp/(1-p)\), \(\operatorname{Var}(N)=rp/(1-p)^2\).
  • Law of total expectation: \(E[X]=E[E[X|Y]]\).
  • Covariance by conditioning: \(\operatorname{Cov}(N,X)=\operatorname{Cov}(N,E[X|N])\) when conditional noise has zero covariance with \(N\).
  • Ito isometry: \(E[(\int_0^T H_s\,dW_s)^2]=E[\int_0^T H_s^2ds]\).
  • Linear ODE \(m'=A+km\): if \(k\ne0\), \(m(t)=m(0)e^{kt}+\frac Ak(e^{kt}-1)\).
